Day 1

The Boy With the Quiet Hands

dawn to dark · Iwagakure, the Hidden Stone

In the grey before dawn, his mother already at her wheel, Iwao does the thing she taught him when he was small and frightened of his own hands. He laces the Ram seal and three threads rise into his palm at once — earth-brown, wind-pale, ember-red — and do not so much as tremble. Control was never the problem. It is everything the control is for that frightens the people who love him.

First period. Asked to cut a single leaf with one nature, he cuts it with three, and it falls open in four clean pieces. The room goes quiet.

Ryūen-sensei"Earth, Wind, and Fire. In a child."

He says it the way other men say the names of the dead. At the back of the room, a broad boy named Ganryū snaps his pencil clean in half and does not notice he has done it.

Then the conditioning stair, and the gift deserts him. His hands can split a leaf into the future; his legs are only eleven years old. He comes in near the back, lungs full of stone-dust, while Ganryū crests the top barely winded and turns to watch the heir straggle in — and something in his face curdles.

At dinner his father comes home grey with quarry dust, hears of the wind that ate a training dummy, and sets down his bowl with the care of a man who handles heavy things for a living.

Gennai, his father"A bloodline isn't a crown. It's a heavier stone. The only question that's ever mattered is whether the man carrying it crushes the people beside him. So don't."

Day 2

Two Hit Points

the first sanctioned ninja spar

A cannon is only frightening until you learn how little powder it holds. Ryūen lets the gloves off — a supervised match, techniques allowed — and Iwao draws Rōmaru, loud and broad and no pushover, who comes to the circle with a real technique in his hands.

DON  ZUUN—

Rōmaru wins the half-beat. A slab of the yard tears loose into a mud-grey copy of himself, and the copy is already inside Iwao's guard before his mortal legs can answer. The world goes white at the edges. He is left swaying — one more touch from flat on his back in front of the whole class.

Two hit points. No time to wear the boy down the patient way; his body cannot survive a second blow. The only weapon that ends a fight in one motion is far too large for a schoolyard — and the two truths of him collide, and he chooses. Don't crush them, his father's voice. He aims to end the moment, not to break the boy.

FWAaSH—!!

Cutting wind. The earth-clone bursts to gravel; Rōmaru is thrown flat and does not rise — a knockout, not a kill, a half-second before the sensei's shout. Iwao stands swaying over a classmate he very nearly hurt for real, and understands his father all at once and forever.

Rōmaru, rubbing his ribs"Okay. Okay, heir — you're not soft."

Day 3

The Nature He Was Told Not to Touch

the forbidden reach

Behind the equipment shed, alone, the day's terror curdled into resolve, he reaches past wind, past fire, past earth, for the place where all three are meant to fuse — the bloodline itself.

…shhhhk.

It catches wrong. His fingertips go dead and cold, and a patch of the wall behind him no bigger than a coin sighs into grey powder and is simply gone — not broken, unmade. For one heartbeat he tastes what the legend actually is, underneath the glory, and it is not glory at all. It is deletion, and it does not care in the least whose hand it sits in.

He never hears the sensei come. Ryūen sees the numb fingers, the hole in solid stone, and the color goes out of his face.

Ryūen, white to the lips"Never alone. Never unsupervised. Not for years."

Under the anger is naked, old fear. And the reframe the whole story turns on lands in the dirt at eleven years old: his control comes free; the bloodline must be earned.

Day 4

If He Can't Touch It, He'll Read It

the particle frame

Forbidden the bloodline in his hands, Iwao goes at it the only way that has ever obeyed him — with his head. He talks his way into the restricted stacks and reads like a starving boy.

And he finds the key. The fusion isn't sequence — that was his whole mistake behind the shed, threading the natures one after another like the leaf-cut. Dust Release is simultaneity: three natures held in perfect, equal balance in a single instant, a lattice the old texts call the particle frame. It takes most people years. It breaks the ones who rush it. For the first time the legend has a shape he can study — and his mind is the one road the village's impatience cannot pave for him.

Akemi, hauling him out by the collar"Brains need dumplings. You're eleven. Come on."

She makes him be a boy for an hour, and does not pry at the new weight on his chakra. She just keeps him human — the quiet, unglamorous technique she is better at than anyone in his life.

Day 5

The Variable He Couldn't Solve

the tactics drill

Tetsuna splits the class into mock three-genin cells — a marker hidden in the rocks, two cells racing for it, the lesson being that a shinobi almost never works alone. Iwao's cell looks to him to call the play. And Iwao does what Iwao does: he tries to solve it.

He builds a plan the way he builds a chakra thread — every variable pinned, nothing left to chance. He tells Akemi where to sense and exactly when; he tells Tobari where to wait. He does not ask. He does not trust. He treats the two people beside him as factors in an equation he alone can balance — because letting them move where he can't see, can't protect them, is unbearable to a boy who knows what his hands do by accident. The plan meets the broken ground and comes apart. The marker slips through the gap between three people who are not, in any way that counts, a team. Across the field, Ganryū's cell of grinders takes theirs clean.

Akemi, in the dust, not softening it"You lost because you don't trust anybody. You're so scared of what your hands can do that you won't put anything important inside reach of them. Including us."

It lands harder than any sensei's correction, because it is true. The boy terrified of crushing the people beside him has solved that fear by keeping everyone at arm's length — exactly where a teammate cannot be. His gift makes him dangerous; his answer to danger is making him alone; and alone, he realizes, is its own kind of crushing.

Day 6

Three Steps Blind

a shinobi's day, hour by hour

There is an arithmetic frightened people do without being taught it: if I hold everything, nothing I hold can fall. It is a child's arithmetic. It is wrong. And Iwao has been doing it his whole life. At dawn he balances three natures on his thumb like a coin on its edge — resting at the border of the bloodline he's forbidden to cross, learning to look and not step.

At the noon break he finds Akemi and says the one technique that costs more than anything in the scrolls. One syllable.

Iwao"Help."

She doesn't make him explain. That is the mercy of her. She just stands and brushes the grit off her knees.

The drill is ancient and merciless to a boy like him: eyes shut, move only on her voice. He flinches at the first call — his eyes snap open of their own accord. He can cut a leaf into the future and he cannot take four steps in the dark on another person's word, because for four steps the safety of Iwao would be in hands that aren't his. She doesn't get bored and she doesn't get cruel. She just resets, and resets, until her voice stops being a test and becomes a metronome.

At dusk, on the fortieth try, he takes three steps blind and does not flinch and does not look — and her hand catches his sleeve at the edge. For three whole steps he let someone else hold the world. The smallest thing. A hairline crack in a wall a mountain has been building since he was old enough to be afraid.

Gennai, at dinner"You let someone lead? Good. That one's heavier than the bloodline. Carry it anyway."

Day 7

The Seventh Day

the rest day · clay, market, quarry, family

Six days the Hidden Stone drives its children up the cliff-stairs; the seventh it only pretends to rest them. He puts his hands in the clay beside his mother's and fails at it, gloriously — his walls collapse, his rims go lopsided.

Tsuru, watching a third pot slump"You mold chakra like a master and clay like a goat."

It's the first time she's teased him in a week. The clay doesn't care that his hands are a legend. The clay only cares whether he's patient. He isn't, yet. He keeps the goat-pot anyway.

In the market a vendor presses extra into his basket and will take no coin — for the heir, please, it's an honor — an old woman touches his sleeve for luck, two children dare each other to ask if he can really turn a man to dust with a look. He is learning that to be recognized is to become, each time, slightly less a person and more a story other people tell — harder and harder to hear yourself think under.

Behind the cistern, Akemi runs the blind walk again. Today he doesn't flinch at the first call, or the second. Five steps in the dark on her word — then eight — and at the end he stands with his eyes shut and his heart steady, which is the part that astonishes him. The fear hasn't vanished. He has simply stopped letting it drive.

At dusk he climbs to the lip of the quarry where his father works the dust that killed his grandfather — a foreman who breathed stone for forty years until his lungs turned to the very thing he cut. Three generations have given the village its walls a fistful of grey lung at a time. And now the mountain has given the family a boy whose gift is to turn stone, cleanly and completely, to dust. He feels the awful symmetry of it, and does not yet know if it is a debt being paid to his line or one being asked of it.

Ōnoki, on the terrace, hearing the day's report"He went to the quarry. He's looking for the cost. Good — the ones who never look for the cost are the ones who make everyone else pay it."

Day 8

The Body Catches Up by Inches

the week's training pays off · the evaluation is announced

Growth is the only jutsu nobody can see being cast. At dawn he holds the three natures a full breath past last week's limit, and they don't gutter. Eight days of cliff-stairs and small-nature drilling and blind walks have begun, by inches, to let his body catch up to the gift it carries. The boy who swayed at two hit points after one spar looks, this morning, like someone a single blow would no longer fold.

The headmaster interrupts first period to announce the mid-term evaluation — twelve days out, graded blind where possible, by examiners who do not care whose blood runs in whose veins.

Sōun, looking at no one in particular"A bloodline buys you nothing in my hall."

Iwao finds, to his surprise, that he is grateful. It is the first time all week an adult has promised to judge him by what he does.

Midday: a stealth stalk across the broken ground. Quiet has always come easy to a boy who spent his life trying not to be noticed by a village that noticed nothing else. He reaches the objective unseen — and a classmate breathes, the cannon can disappear?, with real alarm, as if a mountain had picked itself up and tiptoed. A weapon nobody sees coming frightens people more than one they can.

At dusk, the blind walk reaches ten steps — and at the tenth her hand does not catch his sleeve. Just open ground. He stands in the dark, perfectly balanced, having walked ten whole steps trusting the world is where someone he cares about says it is.

Akemi, grinning"Tomorrow you lead, and I close my eyes."

On the terrace, the old man receives the night's report differently. The thing he set himself to confirm — that the bloodline would not rot in a careless vessel — he has confirmed. A door closes in his chest, quietly satisfied, and behind it a smaller, more dangerous one opens: I should like to see him myself. Soon. Before they make me decide what he's for.

Day 9

Now You Lead

trust has two ends · the cell forms

The hardest part of trust is that it has two ends. He has spent a week learning to be led. Today the inverse — and somehow worse: on the climb, Akemi ties her own headband down over her eyes and puts out her hand, trusting him to walk her up a cliff-stair she cannot see.

He cannot control her into safety — he can only describe the world honestly and trust her to move through it. Two steps. There's a lip — lift your foot. Stop. He brings her ten steps across broken ground without a stumble, his heart in his throat the whole way.

Akemi, pulling off the blindfold"See? You can hold something without crushing it. You just have to say true things and let go."

At lunch he does the second hard thing. He finds Tobari — and instead of building the perfect cell in his head and assigning everyone a place, he asks.

Iwao"I'm bad at the team part. But I want to learn it with people I trust, not people I can manage. Will you be in my cell?" Tobari"You stopped trying to do everything yourself. Okay. I'm in."

By lamplight he builds the cell's plan — and catches the old instinct to script every contingency, to make his friends into reliable variables. He sets the pen down and rebuilds it as a list of what each of them is best at and where each will need the others. A worse plan, by the cold measure of control. A better one, by the only measure that matters. He leaves gaps in it on purpose. Leaving gaps feels like learning to breathe out.

Gennai, at dinner"Learning to need people — that's the heaviest stone there is. A man can carry the bloodline alone. He cannot carry a village alone."

Days 10–20

The Mid-Term

the fortnight, and the test that graded the thing you can't teach

A fortnight is long enough to change a boy and short enough that he doesn't notice. Cliff-stairs every dawn, his legs climbing a half-place higher each week; the small natures kept obedient and separate, never reaching for the fusion. But the real training is three children learning to be a cell — bad at it, then less bad, then, one evening when Akemi calls a danger he can't see and he moves on her word without checking, good. He has stopped being the boy who holds everything and started becoming the boy who holds his part and trusts the rest.

The evaluation: a scenario on the broken ground, graders watching with cold blind eyes that don't care whose blood is whose. Iwao plans it well on paper — and the field refuses the paper. The proctor flanks from an angle he didn't weight; his timing shatters on contact. His plan fails, flatly, in front of the graders. A week ago that would have been the end.

But he had left gaps on purpose. Akemi feels the shift and calls it — left, he's flanking left — and he moves without checking. Tobari throws up an earth-clone screen on the line Iwao abandoned, holding the half-second. And Iwao stops trying to be the genius who saves it and becomes the third point of a frame: earth, wind, and the still center, none of them winning, the whole thing holding. They take the package — not cleanly, not by his plan, but together, through its wreck.

Sōun, in a margin no genin will see"The other boy soloed it brilliantly. This one failed his plan and passed anyway, because three children trusted each other. Put that on the Tsuchikage's desk."

He failed his plan. He passed his test. He understands at last the creed nobody in the Hidden Stone says aloud because they all simply live it: carry the weight together, and never crush the people you carry it beside. A better thing than the bloodline. The thing the bloodline is for.

Day 23

The Awakening That Wasn't

deep in the mountain · the Third Tsuchikage, and the bloodline's refusal

Every story about a prodigy and his birthright tells the same lie: that the gift, when it comes, comes like sunrise — clean, inevitable, deserved. Summoned deep into the mountain, Iwao meets the living legend whose blood he carries: the Third Tsuchikage, no taller than himself and immeasurably heavier.

Ōnoki"Reach for it now. Properly. I am here to catch what you drop. Show me the dust, heir."

And Iwao reaches with everything — the particle frame perfected, the body grown to hold it, the master at his shoulder. He is ready. He closes his hand on the fusion—

GRRRK—NNN

—and it all goes wrong. The natures don't fuse; they collide. A wild, lightless corona of grey nothing blooms from his hands and begins to eat the air, the stone, the edge of Iwao himself — until a small hand closes over his, and Ōnoki's own perfect Dust Release simply devours the boy's failed Jinton whole, faster than fear. A smooth hemisphere is scooped from the stone bench. Iwao is unharmed in body, hollowed in spirit. He reached for his birthright before his idol, and it refused him.

He waits for disappointment. It doesn't come.

Ōnoki"You hold them like a fist. You must hold them like a frame — each nature its own self, trusting the others to pull back, the dust living in the tension where you do not grip at all. You cannot crush a thing into obedience and call it yours."

It is the same lesson the sensor girl taught him, wearing different clothes. The road to the bloodline runs straight through the road he is already walking. Not control. Trust — even of the fire in his own hands. The awakening is deferred. The key is finally in his hand.

Days 24–28

Graduation

the end of the beginning

He doesn't reach for the bloodline again — Ōnoki said years, and meant it. Instead he pours the cavern's lesson sideways into everything that isn't Dust: holding his three natures lightly, his cell lightly, even his own fear lightly. He passes the final exams the way water passes a sieve. Nobody whispers the wind did it anymore. They've watched him work for a year. They know the work is his.

Tetsuna, tying on the headband"You were the easiest student to teach and the hardest to raise. You're raised. Go."

The squads are read blind to blood, by the only logic that builds a cell that survives — not who is strongest, but who balances. Iwao is placed with the two people who spent the year teaching him the lesson the bloodline itself demands: Akemi, his anchor, and Ganryū, his rival — the grindstone, now turned to point the same way he does.

Their jōnin-sensei waits on the cliff and does not come down. They climb to her. Kurikara is scarred from a war the village won't discuss, and looks at the heir with a refreshing, total absence of awe.

Kurikara"On my team you are not a legend. You are a tool I have to keep from breaking the other two. Prove you're more than a bloodline and a liability, and I'll teach you to be a shinobi. We start at dawn. Don't be a prodigy about it. Be on time."

His mother cries; his father doesn't, which costs him more. Gennai presses a small chip of grey stone into Iwao's palm — for the field, so the work and the home stay tied. A headband on the table, a chip of rock in his fist: a genin of the Hidden Stone. The easy part — where the hardest thing he could fail was a grade — is over. And above, an old man turns at last to a longer question: what is the boy for?

Days 29–45

The Work of Genin

D-ranks, and the forging of a team

Nobody tells you that becoming a shinobi mostly means doing chores with your life on the line. Rockslides, runaway goats, tedious caravan escorts — and Kurikara's merciless dawns, running them until the gift meant nothing, because on the day your chakra runs out the only thing left is whether your legs keep moving and whether the people beside you trust you to keep moving with them.

Kurikara, singling out the heir"The strong ones learn to lean last — and they're the ones who get their teammates killed learning it."

He leans. By the fifteenth dawn he fights back-to-back with Ganryū without watching him, trusting the earth-walls to be where his rival says. The year of resentment burns off and leaves respect with its sleeves rolled up — a friendship neither boy will admit to. He is becoming the thing Kurikara is forging: not a cannon, not a prodigy, but a teammate who happens to carry a legend. The trust-the-natures lesson moves through him sideways, the way water moves through stone.

On the forty-fifth day, another caravan escort on the eastern road — the road the merchants have been too scared to use. Kurikara reads the scroll and goes very still.

Kurikara"That's not bandits. Bandits rob one cart and run. Somebody's holding the road. If it goes bad, Iwao — you protect THEM. Not the carts. Not the village's opinion of you. Them."

They set out into a morning that smells like rain and something underneath the rain. The easy part of being a genin comes quietly to its end.

Day 46

The Frame Holds

the eastern road · the true awakening

Ōnoki said the dust would not come like a sunrise. It comes like a door opening in a burning house — not because Iwao reaches for it, but because someone he loves is on the wrong side of the fire. The thing holding the road is a missing-nin, Kettō, a tier above three children. Kurikara is dragged into her own duel. Akemi blinds him with dust-wind and calls his position — perfect, and not enough. Kettō slams the road and Earth Release: Rock Tank throws Ganryū twenty feet. The rival, the grindstone, the friend who'd never call it friendship — down, bleeding, eleven breaths from gone.

The old cold panic rises: seize it, control everything, do it yourself. But this time it meets a year of other lessons in its way — Akemi's steady voice, Ganryū's sacrifice, Kurikara's order (protect them), and a small hand closing over his in a cavern: hold them like a frame, not a fist. So he stops trying to hold anything. He lets earth be earth, wind be wind, fire be fire — and in the still point between them, where his hand grips nothing at all, the particle frame assembles itself out of the air like it had been waiting his whole life for him to stop strangling it.

— FWMMM —

Dust Release: Lesser Detachment. Not the wild grey of the cavern — a clean lattice, controlled, aimed. Where it touches Kettō a piece of the man simply stops being matter. Only a flicker of the legend. It does not need to be more. The Road-Holder looks at the deleted place where his shoulder was, then at the child with the slashed-rock headband and the open hand, and every shinobi's knowledge of exactly one family screams at him — the Tsuchikage's bloodline, in a genin — and he breaks, and runs. The road is held.

Ganryū, around the pain"You used the scary one. On him. For me. …Okay, heir. Okay."
Kurikara, over her wounded student"You held it. And you used it to pick him up instead of to win. That's the whole job, kid. That's the only part that was ever hard."

On the terrace, the old man hears it all, and the question he has carried since the first night — what is the boy for? — comes up at last with an answer he can live with. "He woke it not to win a fight, but to carry a friend out of one. Good. Then he is for the right thing." Then, because he is a Kage: "Put him forward for the exam."

Days 47–58

The Exhibition

the dust shown to the world

A village cannot keep a thing like the dust secret — secrets that large leak through stone. So the Tsuchikage does not hide the heir; he displays him. Ōnoki comes down from his terrace to teach the boy himself: hold the frame; now make it smaller; now make it last. The wild grey of the first reach steadies into a scalpel — Dust Release: Honed Detachment.

At the Exhibition, on a stone effigy, Iwao shows the village the dust — open-handed, gripping nothing — and the effigy's heart simply ceases to be, a perfect cored hollow, the rest untouched. Control, not the army-grinding horror of the legend. A grandmother weeps. A child whispers the Third Tsuchikage's name, and for the first time in Iwao's life it doesn't feel like a weight pressing him flat. It feels like a torch handed forward.

But the foreign eyes in the crowd don't weep. They calculate. A dead bloodline, alive again in a child. By nightfall fast birds leave for capitals that would like to know whether it can be bought, stolen, copied — or killed before it grows.

Kurikara"They'll all be watching you. Some will want to beat you. A few will want you dead before you're grown. Better you learn what that feels like in an exam, where the proctors must stop the killing — than on a battlefield, where they must start it. Carry the dust like it's ours, not yours."

The scroll comes down with the Tsuchikage's seal: Team Kurikara is entered in the Chūnin Selection Exam — the joint, multi-village proving ground, part tournament, part diplomacy, part the only sanctioned place the Five Nations measure each other's young blades short of war. The dust is now a target painted on three backs.

Days 61–70

The Chūnin Exam: Paper & Wasteland

stages one and two · the foreign blades come for the dust

The Five Nations send their young blades to one another's villages and call it an examination. It is partly that. It is also a market, a parade of threats, and a place where wars are quietly decided before anyone draws steel. This cycle the Hidden Stone hosts — so the foreign genin come up the cliff-stairs to where the dust was reborn, and look at Iwao the way buyers look at a horse, or a bomb.

Stage One, the paper: a trap. The questions are beyond any genin; the real test is the tenth — quit now safely, or stay and risk being barred from the rank forever. Iwao, honest to a fault, cannot answer and will not cheat, and sits over a blank page facing disqualification. Then he looks at Ganryū on his left and Akemi on his right, and stays. The exam was never measuring what he knew — only whether he'd be the one who quit on the people beside him. The courage was the answer.

Stage Two, the wasteland: teams hunt teams for scrolls; the truce doesn't reach here. A foreign cell comes for him — for the dust. Akemi blinds them, Ganryū splits them, and when their lightning-quick scroll-hunter comes straight for Iwao, he holds the frame — unhurried, trusting his cell bought the half-second — and answers with Dust Release: Honed Detachment. A scalpel where the first was a flicker. The hunter breaks rather than be unmade and yields the scroll. They walk out with both halves and no blood that won't wash off.

Kurikara, before the finals"The finals are one-on-one, in front of every Kage's envoy on the continent, and they are watching the dust. Out there the whole point of you — the team, the frame, the not-being-alone — is the one thing the rules take away. They want to see if the heir is dangerous with no one to hold the other end of the frame. So that's what we train."

A frame needs more than one force, or it isn't a frame — it's a fist again. Two weeks to learn to hold it alone.

Days 85–90

The Finals, and the Shape of Him

the climax · one boy, one ring, the whole continent watching

The stadium is carved into the mountain, and on the day of the finals it holds every envoy of every Kage come to watch children fight and call it diplomacy. Iwao wins his first two bracket matches the way Kurikara taught him to win everything — efficiently, without cruelty, the dust shown only as much as it needs to be. And then the rules take his team away and set him across a ring from Hayato the Lone Blade: everything Iwao might have become if no one had ever taught him better — a prodigy who decided, young, that other people were weight, and learned to carry nothing but himself.

For three rounds Iwao holds the frame alone — the one thing Kurikara swore he couldn't do. He opens the Lone Blade's guard with the Honed Detachment and unmakes pieces of him, clean and aimed, the crowd silent at the sight of the dust controlled. He trusts the three natures themselves to be the other ends of his frame, and it works, and he takes Hayato from fifty to ten, alone, in front of the continent.

But Hayato is a tier of cruelty Iwao never had to be. The earth comes up like the cliff standing. The second time it takes him off his feet; the third time he does not get up. (The dice, honestly: failed the save against Rock Tank, dropped to 0 — unconscious and dying. The proctors stopped the match.) Iwao lost the final. The medics had him breathing before the dust settled. He woke to a crowd cheering a boy who was not him, and felt, under the failure, a strange and unexpected peace.

Ōnoki, the deciding voice on the dais"He fought a lone wolf alone and lost. Some of you call that a reason to wait — you're right, but not for your reasons. His whole gift is a thing held in tension between forces that trust each other. We took away the very shape of him and were surprised he came up short. He is not ready to be a chūnin yet — not because he is weak, but because the lesson he still owes the world is the one we just punished him for knowing. He'll wear the vest. Not today. Soon."

Promotion at the exam is not won; it is judged. The Third Tsuchikage did not promote his own bloodline. He sent the boy home to his mother instead — "He's earned that more than any vest."

Behind the Dust

the engineering journey — bugs found & fixed while playing
Genesis · fix

You couldn't be born the bloodline you needed

create_character rolled affinity/KKG blindly — Dust (Jinton) was effectively unrollable, which broke the whole premise. Fixed in engine source: a kkg param pins the bloodline deterministically. Commit 4253221.

Combat · fix

Why did every NPC only throw punches?

agent_context read a PC-only field, so kitted adversaries always showed "0 jutsu castable" — nudging the DM toward freeform. Fixed so foes surface their real techniques. Commit af5d2be. Confirmed live in Day 2's spar.

Combat · fix (found live)

A stale turn-budget that wouldn't clear

Mid-exam, scene-mode casting got blocked by a "no action left" gate that should only exist inside initiative. Fixed: the action-economy gate now applies only in active combat, and end_combat clears the budget on the way out. Commit 560e96e — discovered because the agent was genuinely trying to cast, not because anyone went looking.

Feature · built to spec

An XP system, added mid-campaign

The engine had no leveling when play began. Built one on request: progression.ts (xpThreshold = 50·(L−1)·L) + an award_xp intent that auto-levels across thresholds. Every level since is the sum of logged, earned awards — small for a quiet milestone, larger for a valiant finals loss. Commit 69ceb9f.

Method

Played, not narrated

Every check, spar, cast, and rest is a real engine roll. Resources deplete and recover; the village's regard for Iwao climbs through a nightly world-tick where the Tsuchikage literally watches over him. Narration never runs ahead of the dice — three times the dice contradicted the plan (a nat-1 deferred the awakening; a failed save lost the finals) and the story bent to them, never the reverse.
