Episode 1 — Heavy

Eli Vance falls out of the sky over Saltgrave with a System only he can see, eight hit points, one shoe, and the Ninth Tide roaring in his chest.

🎧 Narration

Eli Vance was seventeen, five foot seven on a generous day, and the kind of nobody who lost the student-council election to "abstain." He was walking home from school, earbuds in, rehearsing an argument he was never going to have, when the sky directly above him made a sound like the world's largest sheet of paper being torn in half.

Then there was no ground. Then there was a lot of ground, very far below, getting closer.

Then — and this was the part he'd have trouble explaining later — a window opened in the air in front of his eyes. A literal window, glowing soft cyan, hanging in his vision as he plummeted, like the worst-timed pop-up in history.

"WHAT," Eli explained, at the sky.

The Reach

Below him: a town of pale stone and blue banners, a marketplace packed with people in clothes that had never heard of zippers, and at its heart a spring of glowing water throwing light up the walls like a held breath. It was beautiful. He was going to hit it at terminal velocity. The window helpfully updated.

Eight HP. He didn't know what HP was worth here, but eight of anything sounded low, and "terminal velocity" sounded like a number much bigger than eight. Something in his chest — a place that had been empty his whole life — was suddenly roaring, heavy and metal-bright, like a struck bell the size of a planet. The System put a name on it before he could.

SYSTEM · ◈ THE NINTH TIDE
Anomalous Current online: WEIGHT / IRON / GRAVITY
No living teacher exists. No technique known.
Suggested action: do something.

So he did something. He reached for the roaring thing and grabbed, and he thought, with all the desperation eight hit points can buy: lighter. Make me lighter. Stop being a pancake.

SYSTEM · ◈ CONTROL CHECK
Untrained improvisation — Chakra Control
🎲 d20 → 2 (natural 1-class disaster) +5 = 7 vs DC 16 — FAILURE
The Ninth Tide does not make you light. The Ninth Tide does not do light.

The current answered — oh, it answered — it just answered the opposite of the question. He'd asked to fall slower. Instead the world directly beneath him got heavier, a soundless dome of crushing weight slamming down into the market a half-second before he did, flattening stalls in a perfect ring, pressing fruit to paste and pottery to powder, scooping a shallow crater out of solid stone. He landed in the middle of it like a dropped wrench.

SYSTEM
Impact absorbed by self-inflicted crater. HP 8 / 8 (lucky). Status: PRONE.
First lesson logged: your Tide pushes DOWN, not out. It is not a parachute. It is a hammer.

It did not hurt — much — which Eli would later understand was the single luckiest roll of his entire life, because eight hit points and a hundred-foot fall do not usually end with prone and embarrassed. It ended with prone and embarrassed. He lay in his crater, ears ringing, one shoe gone somewhere over the Atlantic of wherever-this-was, staring at a sky that had a hole in it, and started, quietly, to laugh, because the alternative was the other thing.

"I'm Not Adding Boots to It"

The market was screaming. Not at him, exactly — about him. Fallen, the word went up like a charm against weather, people scrambling back from the crater's edge, somebody's grandmother making a warding sign, nobody, absolutely nobody, reaching down to help the wheezing kid in the crater.

Except one.

A girl about his age vaulted the lip of the crater, basket and all, planted herself between Eli and the crowd, and rounded on the whole market with her hands on her hips.

"Oi — back up, all of you. He's already had the worst day of his life, and I'm not adding boots to it. Someone get me a cloak, someone get me water, and if you keep yelling 'Fallen' like that I'll start charging for the drama."

She looked down at Eli, took in the hoodie, the one shoe, the existential crisis, and grinned like she'd found a stray cat in the rain.

"Hi. I'm Tama. You're in Saltgrave, you fell out of the sky, and that's a bold shoe situation. Can you stand, or are we doing the dramatic-carry thing? Either's fine, I'll narrate."
SYSTEM
Bond formed: Tama — Last Lantern (disposition: warm). She decided you're hers. You did nothing to earn this. Accept it.

Eli took her hand. He'd spent seventeen years on Earth being nobody's first choice for anything, and the first person in a brand-new universe had picked him up off the floor before she knew a single thing about him. He decided, right there in the crater, that he liked the Reach more than he had any business liking it.

"There Is No Tide That Does That"

She hauled him up — Prone cleared, the window chirped — and the crowd's terror was curdling into something stranger and worse: awe. Because an old man in a patched coat had shoved to the crater's edge and gone completely, dangerously still.

He was looking at the ring of flattened stone. At the way the weight had pressed down and not out. His tired face was doing something it clearly hadn't done in years.

"That's not a Tide," Master Goro Sef said, very quietly, to no one. "There are five Tides in the world and I have seen every one of them, and there is no Tide that does that." He looked at Eli — at eight hit points and one shoe and a hole in the sky — the way a man looks at a winning lottery ticket he's afraid to touch in case it's a forgery. "Boy. What are you."

"Eli," said Eli. "I'm — Eli. Sorry about your market."

High above the square, in the shaded patron's box that overlooked the great fount, a beautifully-dressed man set down his cup, smiled the warm and famous smile that the whole Reach loved him for, and leaned forward with the patient, delighted interest of someone who has just watched exactly the thing he came to find walk out of the sky and crater it.

SYSTEM
⚠ You are being observed. (Source declines to identify. The smile is doing a lot of work.)

The Hook

The cloak came. The water came. Tama kept up a running commentary that did more to calm Eli's hammering heart than the water did. And Master Goro Sef crouched at the edge of the crater Eli had made, did some arithmetic behind his eyes that ended somewhere between terrified and hopeful, and said the sentence that started everything.

"The Founts' Trials open in nine days. My sect needs an entrant or it loses its charter and its name and its fount, all three." He looked at the impossible crater, then at the impossible boy. "You can't control it, you don't know our world, and you'll probably get yourself killed." A long pause. The unguarded thing escaped him anyway. "Come to the Last Lantern. Let's find out what you are before someone worse does it for you."

Eli looked at the glowing window only he could see, and the old man only he'd been crazy enough to crater a market in front of, and the girl who'd already adopted him, and the hole in the sky that used to be his way home.

"Okay," he said. "Yeah. Okay." He almost smiled. "Do I get a shoe first?"

— He wanted to matter. The sky took him at his word and dropped him somewhere he absolutely does. Nine days to the bell. One shoe. A System nobody else can see, and a power that only knows how to push down. Let's go.
