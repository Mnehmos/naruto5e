A cannon is only frightening until you learn how little powder it holds. Today Iwao would learn it from the inside, with the whole class watching.

The Groove

The second morning already wore a groove. The dawn molding came without effort — three natures up the seal, held and released, his mother's wheel turning behind him; Mai's sparkly hands over rice; the long climb up the switchbacks with Akemi narrating the entire village into his ear whether he wanted it or not. By the time the Academy bell rang he had stopped feeling like a visitor in his own days.

The pattern of him was setting like poured concrete. Godlike in the hands. Painfully mortal everywhere else. Nobody had yet seen what happened when those two truths collided inside the same thirty seconds.

They were about to.

The Circle

Ryūen finally let the gloves come off. A sanctioned match, he announced — techniques permitted, first to a clean decisive blow, and he himself would preside. The class drew lots, and Iwao drew Rōmaru: loud, broad-shouldered, one of Ganryū's orbit, a boy who had been dying for a crack at the heir since the leaf fell into four pieces. He came to the chalk circle cracking his knuckles, and he came with a real technique in his hands, because whatever else Rōmaru was, he was not a pushover.

The bell. They moved at the same instant — and the half-beat fell to Rōmaru.

He slammed his palms to the dirt and a slab of the yard tore loose and rose into a mud-grey copy of himself, and the copy was already moving, already inside Iwao's guard before Iwao's mortal legs could answer. It hit him the way the cliff hits a falling climber.

The world went white at the edges. Iwao found himself still standing, somehow, but only just — swaying, one more touch from flat on his back in the chalk in front of the entire class, his breath gone, his body screaming that it had nothing left to give.

The Two Truths Collide

There was no time. That was the whole, terrible lesson of it. No time to wear Rōmaru down across a careful exchange the way a patient fighter would; no time to be gentle the slow way. His fragile body simply could not survive a second blow, and the only weapon he had that could end a fight in one motion was a weapon far too large for a schoolyard.

So the two truths collided, and Iwao chose.

He breathed his father's words — don't crush them, don't crush them — and aimed not to break the boy but to end the moment. The cutting weasel-wind howled off his fingertips. Rōmaru's earth-clone burst back into loose gravel; Rōmaru himself was lifted off his feet and thrown flat, and did not get up — a half-second, no more, before Ryūen's "STOP!" reached the circle.

The yard was silent. It was a spar; Rōmaru was not dead, only done — but everyone had seen it, and so had Iwao. He stood swaying over a classmate he had very nearly hurt for real, and understood, all at once and forever, the exact thing his father had told him at this same hour the night before. A heavier stone. Whether the man crushes the people beside him. He had come within a breath of it. On the second day.

Wary Respect, and an Empty Tank

Rōmaru woke rubbing his ribs and, to his lasting credit, grinned crooked through the pain. "Okay," he managed. "Okay, heir. You're not soft." It was the first time anyone in that class had looked at Iwao as a person rather than a rumor, and it had cost a boy getting knocked cold to do it.

But the spar had hollowed him out. One exchange — one — and half his chakra was gone and every reserve in his body was spent. He could win any single instant that existed. He could survive almost none of them. He went home early, exactly as he had promised his mother, on legs that felt like wet rope.

The Lesson Comes Due

At dinner he didn't brag. He told them the truth instead — not the win, but how close he had come to the floor, and the wind he'd had to throw to live, and how near Rōmaru's ribs had passed to something that doesn't mend. Tsuru held him without a word. Gennai only nodded, his sentence from the night before made flesh inside a single afternoon.

And somewhere in his mother's arms the boy made the first true decision of his shinobi life. Not to become stronger — strength was the easy gift, the one that came free. He decided to become controlled enough that the strength protected the people standing beside him instead of crushing them. It was a harder technique than anything in the scrolls, and he understood, dimly, that he would spend his whole life learning it.

He slept hard. Above the rooftops the old man on the terrace watched a little longer than the night before, and said nothing at all, and the village leaned one degree further toward the boy who had nearly broken a classmate to keep from falling. None of them had been afraid of that today. Only Iwao had. That difference was the whole of him.
