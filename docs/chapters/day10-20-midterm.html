A fortnight is long enough to change a boy and short enough that he doesn't notice it happening. Iwao spent his the way the Hidden Stone spends everything — slowly, under weight, in repetitions no one claps for.

The Fortnight (Days 10–19)

The cliff-stairs every dawn, and his legs climbing a half-place higher each week, the body grinding up toward the gift the way water grinds up toward the level of its source. The small-nature drills, wind and earth and fire each kept obedient and separate, never reaching for the fusion — not for years, Ryūen's voice, in the hands before the blood. The archive in the gaps, the particle frame sketched until he could draw it with his eyes shut.

But the real training was the one that didn't look like training: three children learning to be a cell. They were bad at it, and then less bad, and then — somewhere around the sixth evening, when Akemi called a danger Iwao hadn't seen and he moved on her word without checking — they were, for three whole seconds, good. He kept leaving gaps in the plans on purpose. Tobari kept filling them with a patience Iwao didn't have. Akemi kept being the eyes in the back of his head he'd spent his life refusing to grow. By Day 19 they could run a course without speaking, and Iwao had stopped being the boy who held everything and started, by inches, becoming the boy who held his part and trusted the rest to hands that were not his.

He grew, in those days, in the quiet way the body keeps score — sturdier, deeper-welled, a reserve that no longer ran dry after a single technique. He did not feel like a cannon with a thimble of powder anymore. He felt, for the first time, like something that could last a fight.

The Mid-Term (Day 20)

08:00 — The Field

The evaluation was a scenario on the broken ground below the Academy: a "package" to retrieve, a proctor playing the threat, graders watching from the cliff with cold blind eyes that did not care whose blood was whose. Three cells ran it in turn. Iwao's went last, which meant he had all morning to do the thing he was worst at, which was plan.

09:30 — The Plan, and the Crack

He planned well, on paper — and then the field, as fields do, refused the paper. The proctor came from an angle his plan hadn't weighted; the timing he'd scripted shattered on contact; and Iwao felt the old cold panic rise, the certainty that he should seize it all, control every variable, do it himself the way he'd done the leaf and the spar. His plan failed. Flatly, completely, in front of the graders. A week ago that failure would have been the end.

09:31 — The Gaps He Left on Purpose

But he had left gaps. And into them moved the cell he had spent a fortnight learning to trust. Akemi felt the proctor's shift before Iwao saw it and called it — left, he's flanking left — and Iwao moved on her word without checking, because he had practiced exactly that a hundred times in a cistern lot with his eyes shut. Tobari threw up an earth-clone screen at the line Iwao had abandoned in his panic, holding the ground Iwao couldn't, buying the half-second. And in that half-second Iwao stopped trying to be the genius who saved it and became, instead, the third point of a frame — earth and wind and the still center, three forces in tension, none of them winning, the whole thing holding. They took the package. Not cleanly. Not by his plan. But together, through the wreck of his plan, which the graders — who had seen a hundred prodigies solo a hundred tests — noted with raised eyebrows was somehow rarer and harder to teach.

11:00 — The Grade That Wasn't on the Sheet

They passed. The marks the examiners wrote were ordinary-good. But Sōun, who had promised to judge the heir by what he did and not whose blood he carried, wrote a second line in a margin no genin would ever see: the Yotsuki boy soloed it brilliantly. This one failed his plan and passed anyway, because three children trusted each other. Put that on the Tsuchikage's desk. It went up the mountain that night, as the old man had asked, and Ōnoki read it twice, and set it down, and looked out over his village for a long time.

19:30 — The Cell, At a Table

That night the three of them ate stewed eel at the stalls and were, for an hour, just children who had passed a hard thing together. Iwao paid, with the twenty Ryo from the belt-pouch his mother had packed his first day, and it felt like the best money he had ever spent. He had failed his plan. He had passed his test. He understood, finally and in his bones, the creed nobody in the Hidden Stone said aloud because they all simply lived it: carry the weight together, and never crush the people you carry it beside. It was, he thought, a better thing than the bloodline. It was the thing the bloodline was for.
