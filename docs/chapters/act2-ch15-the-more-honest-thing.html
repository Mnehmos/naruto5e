Act II, Chapter 15 — The More Honest Thing

Gomei believes a war is the only honest thing left. Iwao has until dawn to prove him wrong — not with an argument but with a thing that actually happens: two villages choosing, for the first time in eighteen years, to dig up the crime they buried.

Argue the cure, not the culprit

The cure for a war built on a buried crime is the crime, confessed by the hands that buried it.

Gomei had spent eighteen years believing there was only one honest thing left in the world, and it was a war. Iwao had until the next dawn to prove him wrong, and the proof could not be a clever argument or a won fight. It had to be a thing that actually happened — something the grieving man in the treeline could watch occur and not be able to call a lie, because he had made a religion out of detecting lies and he would detect any Iwao tried to tell.

So Iwao did not try to tell one. He spent the day trying to make the truth large enough to stand on.

The Case for the Dead

He started with the part he could prove past doubt, because that was how you earned the right to ask for the part you couldn't. The captured proxy — alive, sweating, certain now that the contract had been a death sentence with his name on the back — gave up the cut-out chain he'd been paid through, and it sang the same song as the courtyard striker before him: hired to kill the heir, dressed to blame the Cloud, manufacturing one more incident in a war that kept being built and kept failing to light. That was the easy half. Iwao laid it out clean and let it stand.

Then he reached for the hard half, the one that mattered, and made the argument of his life — not to a crowd, not with charm, but the only way he had ever been any good: as a pattern, undeniable, laid down link by link.

"You can catch every broker and every blade," he told the Tsuchikage, through the proof and the distance and the old man's narrowed eyes, "and there will be another one next spring, and another the spring after, for as long as the wound is down there in the dark for someone to dig up. Gomei isn't the disease. He's the symptom of a buried thing. You can keep treating symptoms with cells and prisons forever — or you can do the one thing that takes the wound away from every future Gomei before he's even angry yet. You can make the Eleven true again. Named. Marked. Admitted. A wound that's been confessed can't be used to recruit a war. Only a buried one can."

And Ōnoki — who had governed in the era that buried them, who knew to the gram what acknowledging this would cost in pride and legitimacy and the comfortable sleep of old men — was quiet for a long time, and then made the decision that Iwao would remember as the bravest he ever watched a powerful man make, because it was the kind that costs the one who makes it and helps the ones who can't pay him back.

"You have made your case with blood, proof, and a harder mercy than most men can bear. I will not pretend the Hidden Stone is innocent if our peace was bought with erased names. If the Eleven of the Hollow Pass were struck from our records, then that wound cannot be allowed to fester another eighteen years in the dark. I will order the archives opened. I will have the graves found, named, and marked. And I will see what can be confessed — without turning this village into a liar that teaches its children lies." The old eyes hardened, not against the boy, but against the easy version of what he'd just agreed to. "But hear me: truth, once loosed, must be held with both hands. We will not let this become another tool for Gomei, nor another blade turned against the living. If Stone is to stand, it will stand on stone that has been cleaned of rot — not hidden beneath it."

The Stone's half was given. It was the half Iwao had the standing to ask for. The other half belonged to the people the Eleven had actually been, and Iwao could not ask the Cloud for anything; he could only ask the one Cloud man who had already, once, chosen the harder honesty over his own comfort.

The Cloud's Half, Carried Not Commanded

Raido heard the whole shape of it — the Stone confessing, the graves to be marked, the cycle to be broken at the root — and Iwao watched him understand the asymmetry of what came next. Because the Eleven had not only been murdered by the Stone. They had been abandoned by the Cloud, disavowed by their own command the same week, thrown away to keep a clean accord. For the acknowledgment to actually close the wound — for it to be a healing and not just the Stone performing penance while Kumo kept its silence — the Cloud had to un-erase its own. And no envoy could command a village to confess that it had discarded its own children.

"You ask a hard kindness," Raido said, "and you ask it honestly." He did not look away from it. "I will carry it home myself — not as command, but as witness: the Stone has chosen to name its dead and its fault, and the Cloud should not leave the ledger one-sided if it means to heal rather than wound again. I cannot promise my village will confess as swiftly as your Tsuchikage has. But I can promise my voice, my standing, and the proof I have seen with my own eyes. If Kumo abandoned its own — then that shame is ours to meet." A small, grim nod. "Show me what you are willing to let be recorded, and I will take it to the Cloud without softening the edge."

The hard half, honestly received: not a guarantee — he can't make the Cloud confess on schedule — but a true commitment of his own voice and standing to carry the un-softened truth home. Both halves of the acknowledgment are now in motion. Honest, uncertain, real.

It was not a treaty. It was not even a promise that would certainly be kept. It was two villages, for the first time in eighteen years, turning toward the thing they had agreed to bury instead of away from it — the Stone by order, the Cloud by the conscience of one good envoy who would spend himself carrying it. It was, Iwao thought, the most fragile and the most real thing he had ever helped make. A buried wound, dug up on purpose, by the hands that buried it. The exact opposite of everything the Hollow Pass had ever taught anyone.

Which left only the man it had taught hardest.

The Letter to the Treeline

He could not summon Gomei. You do not summon a man out of eighteen years of silence; a summons is just another order from the kind of authority that erased him, and he would read it as a trap and be right to. And Iwao could not trap him — a trap would prove, finally and forever, that the boy was the same as everyone who had ever stood on these rocks and chosen the dishonest, killing thing.

So he decided, lying awake under the unwalled sky for the second night, to do the only thing that fit. He would not send a letter. He would be one. At first light he would carry the proof that the impossible thing was actually happening — Ōnoki's sealed order to open the archives, Raido's pledge to carry the Cloud's half — down to the eleven cairns Gomei had tended for eighteen years. He would set the watch back, send the cell to the ridge, and stand alone at the graves in the open, unhidden, holding the door wide, and wait.

He would let the grieving man choose to come, or not. Because that was the whole difference between what Iwao was offering and what had been done to the Eleven: the Eleven had not been given a choice. Gomei would be.

"It might not work," Akemi said quietly, on the ridge, when he told her the plan and the part where he stood alone. "He's had eighteen years to decide the only door is a war. You can't argue someone out of a grief that big in one morning."

"I'm not going to argue," Iwao said. He looked down at the cairns in the dark, eleven small patient shapes, and the chipped iron cup at the foot of one of them, polished by a dead boy's only friend. "I'm just going to stand where his friends are buried and tell him the truth is coming up out of the ground at last, and that he doesn't have to set the world on fire to make anyone remember them. And then I'm going to do the hardest thing I've ever done." He breathed out. "I'm going to let him decide, and not move my hands, no matter what he decides."

Up the slope, in the dark beyond the firelight that wasn't lit, the watcher held his treeline and his silence, and somewhere in the long cold hours a man who had not had a choice in eighteen years felt one being built for him, stone by stone, by a boy who would not stop, and did not yet know what he would do with it when the dawn finally offered it to him.

— He could not out-argue a war or out-fight a grief, so he made the truth large enough to stand on: the Stone will name its dead, the Cloud will carry its shame home, and at dawn the lens will stand alone at eleven graves and offer a door instead of a blade.
