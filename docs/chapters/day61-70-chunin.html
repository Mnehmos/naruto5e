Days 61–70 — The Chūnin Exam: Paper and Wasteland

Paper and wasteland: passed on the courage of the tenth question, then a controlled Honed Detachment routs a foreign hunter sent for the dust.

The Five Nations send their young blades to one another's villages on a schedule, under a truce older than any current grudge, and call it an examination. It is partly that. It is also a market, a parade of threats, and a place where wars are quietly decided before anyone draws steel. This cycle the Hidden Stone was host, which meant the foreign genin came up the cliff-stairs to where the dust had been reborn — and looked at Iwao the way buyers look at a horse, or a bomb.

Stage One — The Paper (Day 61)

The written exam was a trap, as it always is. The questions were deliberately beyond a genin; the real test was whether you would cheat cleverly enough to survive, and whether, at the end, you had the nerve to stay. Iwao — honest to a fault, a scholar who could not bring himself to steal answers — sat over a page he could not fill and felt the old Day-One shame of the blank sheet rising. (Ninshou 12 vs DC 15 — he could not answer, and would not cheat.)

And then the proctor asked the tenth question, which was not a question at all: quit now, safely, with your team — or stay, and risk being barred from the rank forever. Half the room broke and left. Iwao looked at Ganryū on his left and Akemi on his right, and understood that the exam had never been measuring what he knew. It was measuring whether he would be the one who quit on the people beside him. He set down his pencil over the blank page and stayed. The courage was the answer. Team Kurikara advanced — not on what Iwao knew, but on what he would not do.

Stage Two — The Wasteland (Days 63–69)

The survival stage turned the genin loose into the dead stone country east of the village with half a scroll each and a week to take the other half off someone else. Teams hunted teams. The truce did not reach here; the proctors were obligated to retrieve bodies, not prevent them.

Kurikara had drilled them for exactly this, and it showed. They moved as one animal — Akemi's sensing turning the wasteland transparent, Ganryū's earth raising walls and false trails, Iwao holding the center. They were hunted twice and slipped both nets. And on the sixth day a foreign cell came for them specifically — for him, for the dust, three genin led by a lightning-quick scroll-hunter named Raijū who had been sent, it was plain, less to pass the exam than to test the reborn bloodline's edge.

He found that edge. Akemi blinded his cell with dust-wind; Ganryū split them with a wall; and when Raijū came through it straight for Iwao, fast as his namesake, Iwao held the frame — open-handed, unhurried, trusting his cell to have bought him the half-second — and answered with Dust Release: Honed Detachment. Clean. Aimed. A scalpel where the Lesser Detachment had been a flicker. (30 force damage; Raijū dropped to a sliver, broke, and yielded the scroll rather than be unmade.) The foreign cell took their wounded and fled, and somewhere on a distant cliff an observer made a note that the dust was not only awake — it was controlled, which was far more frightening.

Team Kurikara walked out of the wasteland on the ninth day with both scroll-halves and no blood on their hands that wouldn't wash off. They had passed stage two. And Iwao had grown — the wasteland and the fight and the long nights of holding watch for his sleeping teammates had filled him out into something a missing-nin would no longer mistake for a soft target. (Iwao now L6: HP 38, Chakra 59.)

Between Stages (Day 70)

There would be a delay before the finals — the proctors needed to thin the field, and the village needed to seat the dignitaries who had come to watch young blades cut each other in a stadium and call it diplomacy. Kurikara gathered her three on the cliff and, for once, did not insult them.

"You passed the paper by not quitting and the wasteland by not breaking," she said. "The finals are different. The finals are one-on-one, in front of every Kage's envoy on the continent, and they are watching the dust." She looked at Iwao. "Out there you will be alone in the ring, and the whole point of you — the team, the frame, the not-being-alone — will be the one thing the rules take away. They'll want to see if the heir is still dangerous when there's no one to hold the other end of the frame." She let that land. "So that's what we train, these next two weeks. How you hold the frame when you're the only one holding it."

Iwao looked at the small grey chip of stone his father had given him for the field, turning it over in his fingers, and thought: a frame needs more than one force, or it isn't a frame, it's a fist again. He did not yet know how he would hold three natures in balance alone, in a ring, with the continent watching. But he had two weeks, and a sensei who had buried enough prodigies to teach him, and the slow Hidden-Stone certainty that you solve the cliff one step at a time.
