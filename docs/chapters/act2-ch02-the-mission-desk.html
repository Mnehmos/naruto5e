Act II, Chapter 2 — The Mission Desk

A clerk's day at the mission desk — and the eastern-road reports don't add up. Iwao sees the thing the village has decided not to know yet.

The world the DM didn't script

Both NPCs — Raido the Cloud envoy and Kazan the mission clerk — took their own turns: real model declarations conformed and resolved through the engine. The DM set the pressure; the characters chose their own words.

A village runs on two things: the shinobi who go out, and the paper that says where they went. Iwao had spent his whole life imagining he would be the first kind. No one had warned him that the road to the first kind ran, for a while, straight through the second.

Dawn, the Open Hand

He woke before the bell and did the thing his mother taught him — sat, breathed, laced the Ram seal, drew up the three threads and held them in an open palm, doing nothing. Eleven days since the finals; the habit had changed shape. He no longer held them to prove he could. He held them to remember he didn't have to. Frame, not fist. Mai mumbled in her sleep and stole the blanket. He let her. Some things you do not grip.

The Gate, and the Pleasant Man

The cliff-stairs were grey and endless and honest, and Iwao climbed them with a Genin's empty morning ahead of him — clerical assist work, the paper kind of duty. He was most of the way up when a man fell into step beside him, smooth as a door swinging open on oiled hinges.

He wore Cloud grey under a traveler's coat, and an envoy's papers folded in two fingers, and a smile that had been measured to the millimeter.

"Good morning. Your ascent is impressive for such a young shinobi." A small bow, perfectly weighted. "Might I walk beside you a moment? I'm Raido, of the Cloud. I'd be pleased to hear how Iwagakure is treating its promising young talent today."

Every word was a kindness and every kindness was a ruler laid against him. How is the village treating its talent — which meant are you the weapon we heard about, which meant is it awake. Iwao had recited the deeds of foreign sensors in a classroom; he knew the shape of a man taking a reading. He gave the envoy a boy's shrug and a boy's tired answer — they have me filing papers, sir — and watched something flicker behind the pleasant eyes that might have been disappointment and might have been a note taken. The Cloud did not climb a mountain to hear that the heir filed papers. Which meant the Cloud would climb it again.

He carried the small cold weight of being read the rest of the way up.

The Desk

Kazan ran the mission desk the way some men run a dam — by making very sure no one downstream ever heard the water. He was tired in the bones and kind in the way tired men are kind, which is to say efficiently, and he took one look at the dust-heir reporting for assist duty and saw, with visible relief, a strong back.

"You're the famous one," he said, not unkindly. "Good. The famous one can carry. Those stacks to the archive, sorted by road and moon. Try not to be a legend about it."

So Iwao carried. It was honest work and it was beneath him and it was, he understood somewhere in the second hour, exactly the point — the village setting its heaviest stone to lifting the lightest things, to see whether he'd sulk at the weight of being small. He didn't sulk. He sorted by road and moon, and he read while he sorted, because he had always read everything, and that was Kazan's first mistake.

The Counts That Wouldn't Match

It was a small wrongness, the kind you feel before you can name. The eastern-road ledgers had a rhythm, and the rhythm skipped.

He laid them side by side on the archive floor: more caravans logged as departed than ever logged arrived. Not many. Enough. And several of the arrival reports were written in too neat a hand — recopied, the original tallies gone, the new counts rounded into a tidiness that ledgers kept by tired road-clerks never have. Someone had been combing the numbers smooth. Someone had decided the eastern road was bleeding caravans and that the kindest thing to do with that fact was to make it quieter.

He carried one of the recopied sheets back to the desk and set it down and asked Kazan, plainly, the way an honest boy asks, why the numbers didn't match.

The room did not go silent the way classrooms had gone silent for him. It went careful.

"Keep your voice down, Iwao." Kazan didn't look up; his pen kept moving, which was its own answer. "Put those on the desk and let me sort the duplicates from the clean copies. If the counts still don't match after that — we'll flag it through the proper channel. Not across the room."

It was not a lie. That was the thing that stayed under Iwao's ribs the rest of the day. It was not a lie — it was a man who had decided that calm was a kind of mercy and paperwork was a kind of wall, and who was not wrong, exactly, and who was building the wall anyway. Duplicates and clean copies. Maybe. Or maybe the eastern road was eating caravans and a tired good man upstream was holding back the water so the village wouldn't hear it rise.

Iwao put the sheet down. He did not push. He was a Genin; pushing was not his to do. But he had read the numbers, and you cannot un-read a number, and he carried that weight home too — heavier than the stacks, lighter than it would become.

Dinner, and the Weight You Don't Set Down

His father came home grey with quarry-dust and listened to the day over the stone-pot, and when Iwao got to the part about the counts that wouldn't match, Gennai stopped chewing.

"A road that loses caravans," he said slowly, "is a road somebody is taking them from. And a desk that tidies the numbers is a desk that's decided not to know yet." He set his bowl down with the great care of a man who handled heavy things. "You're a Genin. It's not your road to fix. But you've got eyes that read, boy, and that's a thing the village will reach for before it reaches for your blood. Be careful which they ask of you first."

Tsuru said nothing, and turned her wheel, and watched her son hold a thing in an open hand that he could not put down and could not yet lift.

Above the village, on his terrace, a small old man received a quiet report that the heir had been set to carry paper and had, instead of carrying it, read it — and had asked the desk a question the desk did not want asked. Ōnoki folded his hands. Good, he thought. Eyes before blood. Let us see what he does with a thing he is too small to fix.

And far to the east, past the last waystation, in the treeline where the road bent blind, a patient man counted crates by moonlight and was pleased that the village had not yet begun to count them too.
