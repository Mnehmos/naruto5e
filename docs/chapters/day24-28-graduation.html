You do not feel an era end. You feel a Tuesday end, and only later, looking back, do you see that the Tuesday was the last one of something.

The Last Days (Days 24–27)

The cavern stayed in him. Hold them like a frame, not a fist. Iwao did not reach for the bloodline again — Vohl had said years, and meant it, and Iwao had stopped being a boy in a hurry to be the legend the village wanted. Instead he poured the lesson sideways, into everything that wasn't Particle art; he held his three separate natures more lightly now, trusting each to be itself; he held his cell more lightly, asking instead of commanding; he held even his own fear more lightly, the way you hold a coal you've decided to stop being burned by. The final practical exams came and went. He passed them the way water passes a sieve — wind cut clean, earth shaped true, fire kept to its leash, every technique disciplined where a year ago it would have been merely large. Top marks. Nobody whispered the wind did it anymore. They had watched him work for a year. They knew, now, that the work was his.

Ganryū graduated two places below him and did not seem to mind, which was the strangest development of all. The broad boy had spent the year out-working a prodigy and discovering, slowly, that the prodigy was out-working him back — and somewhere in the mutual grinding the hatred had worn down to a whetstone's purpose. Rivals, it turned out, was a kind of friendship that refused to admit it.

Graduation Day (Day 28)

Morning — The Headband

They lined up in the yard where, a year ago, Iwao had cut a leaf into four pieces and frightened a room. Tetsuna — who had never once smiled at him, which he had come to understand was her highest form of respect — tied the Hidden Tor's headband around his brow with hands like a retaining wall, and said the only soft thing she would ever say to him: "You were the easiest student to teach and the hardest to raise. You're raised. Go." The slashed-rock symbol sat cool against his forehead, the weight of a village he now officially carried.

Midday — The Assignment

Then the part that mattered. Headmaster Sōun read the squads, blind to blood as he'd promised, by the only logic that ever builds a cell that survives: not who is strongest, but who balances. And the logic put Iwao on a team with the two people who had spent the year teaching him the lesson the bloodline itself demanded — Akemi, his anchor, the voice he had learned to move on in the dark; and Ganryū, his rival, the grindstone that had sharpened him, now turned to point the same direction he did. Tobari went to another cell, and there was a pang in it, and also a rightness — you do not get to keep everyone; you get to trust the ones the work puts beside you.

Afternoon — Kurikara

Their jōnin-sensei was waiting on the cliff above the yard, and she did not come down to meet them. They climbed to her. Kurikara was scarred down one side of her face from a war the village did not talk about, and she looked at the three of them with an absolute, refreshing absence of awe. She looked longest at Iwao, and what she said, she said flat:

"I don't care whose blood you carry. The village cares. I buried better shinobi than you'll ever be because somebody upstairs cared more about their bloodline than their teammates." She let that sit. "On my team you are not a legend. You are a tool I have to keep from breaking the other two. Akemi tells me you've started to learn that. Good. Started." She turned to go, then added, over her shoulder, the first thing that sounded like a door left open: "Prove you're more than a bloodline and a liability, and I'll teach you to be a shinobi. We start at dawn. Don't be a prodigy about it. Be on time."

Evening — The Stoneware House

His mother cried, which she had sworn she would not, and his father did not, which cost him more visibly. Gennai set a fresh chip of grey stone on the windowsill and a second, smaller one in Iwao's palm — for the field, he said, so the work and the home stay tied. Mai demanded the sparkly hands and got them, brighter than usual, a small private fireworks for a sister who would not understand for years what her brother had become. And Iwao sat in the house that smelled of clay and stone, a genin of the Hidden Tor, a headband on the table and a chip of grey rock in his fist, and understood that the easy part — the part where the hardest thing he could fail was a grade — was over.

Somewhere above, an old man received word that the heir had graduated, top marks, assigned to Kurikara — good, she won't coddle him — and turned his attention, for the first time, to a different and longer question, the one he had named in the dark on the very first night: what is the boy for? The mountain had finished raising the seed. Now it would begin, carefully, to find out what it had grown.
