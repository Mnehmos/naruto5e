Day 6 — Three Steps Blind

Three steps blind: the prodigy who fears his own hands is made to trust the people beside him — the wound that becomes his whole arc.

🎧 Narration

There is an arithmetic frightened people do without ever being taught it. It goes: if I hold everything, nothing I hold can fall. It is a child's arithmetic. It is wrong. And it is almost impossible to unlearn, because for a long time it appears to work.

05:31 — The Edge of a Coin

He surfaced before the light, the way he always did now, and folded the Ram before his eyes were fully open. Earth, wind, fire — three threads up onto the pad of his thumb, balanced there like a coin stood on its edge. He did not reach past them. He had learned, in the days since the wall behind the shed, to simply rest in the balance of the three, the way you lay a flat hand on a sleeping animal to feel it breathe. The fusion lived past that balance, in a country he was forbidden to enter. He had learned to stand at the border and look across, and not step.

06:04 — The Chip of Grey Stone

Tsuru set rice in front of him and watched him climb up out of the trance with that careful blankness she had worn for a week — a mother doing her own frightened arithmetic about her son and reaching the same unbearable answer. On the windowsill sat a chip of grey stone; his father left one there every morning and carried one out to the quarry, a mason's small superstition Iwao had never asked about, a way of keeping the work and the home tied by a thread of the same mountain. Mai climbed his knee demanding the sparkly hands. He walked a single thread of light across his knuckles, smaller than usual, and she laughed the unguarded laugh of someone who has never once been afraid of him, and for the length of that laugh the arithmetic let go of his chest. He pocketed the grey chip on the way out without deciding to. It sat cool against his hip all day.

07:00 — The Climb

Akemi fell into step on the switchbacks, uninvited as ever, narrating the whole waking village into his ear — whose stall was opening, whose dog had gotten loose, which jōnin had come back grey-faced from the border road in the night. He had started, lately, actually listening. She noticed things the way he noticed seams in a genjutsu, and somewhere in the last week he had stopped finding it noise and started finding it a map.

08:00 — First Period (Chakra Control)

Tetsuna no longer watched him mold; that was its own grade. The lesson was leaf-cutting again, and Iwao cut his cleanly and then spent the rest of the period helping the boy beside him find the angle of his own chakra — a thing the old Iwao would never have done, because helping meant slowing down, and slowing down meant trusting the work would still be there when he looked back at it.

09:32 — Second Period (Conditioning)

The cliff-stair, packs and lungs and no chakra allowed. Middle of the pack, the taste of stone-dust, the burn. Ganryū three places ahead and — this was new — not turning around to gloat. Just running, grimly, the way a man runs who has decided to out-work the weather itself. Iwao watched the broad back pull away and felt something that was not quite envy and not quite respect, and had no name for it yet.

11:03 — The Word That Cost the Most

At the break he found Akemi where the sensors always sat — backs to something solid; he had started noticing things like that — and he said the technique that cost him more than anything in the scrolls. One syllable. "Help." She did not make him explain. That was the mercy of her. She just brushed the grit off her knees and stood up.

12:00 — Lunch

Tobari, who rarely spoke, slid half a pickled plum across the bench to him and said only, "You looked like you needed it," which was either about the plum or about the morning, and Iwao decided not to ask which. Suzume sharpened a kunai and watched the yard the way she always did, taking inventory of everyone, and gave Iwao the smallest nod, which from Suzume was a parade.

13:00 — Third Period (History & Strategy)

The Second Stoneward's border campaigns — the logistics of feeding an army across dead stone, where the real war is fought against thirst and distance before a single blade is drawn. Iwao answered three questions right and felt the small familiar shame of the answers coming too easily, like the leaf. But he also caught himself sketching, in the margin, three supply lines balanced against one another so no one line could be cut without the others holding — and only later realized he had drawn the particle frame again, in a different language.

14:30 — Fourth Period (Bukijutsu)

Kanna set them throwing. Iwao's hands did their uncanny thing and he buried three in a tight triangle, and then — deliberately, an experiment — he closed his eyes and threw, and missed the target entirely, and felt strangely better for the missing. He was beginning to suspect that everything that came free to him came free because it lived in his eyes and his hands, and that the things that mattered most lived somewhere he couldn't see.

16:00 — The Drill

Which was the whole afternoon. Eyes shut. Move only on my voice. A sensor leading a blind partner through a course only she could feel — the oldest cell-drill there is, and merciless to a boy like him. Left. Two steps. Stop. Post on your right — go around. He flinched at the first call. His eyes snapped open of their own accord, and there was the post, exactly where she'd said, and there was Akemi, not laughing. He could split a leaf into the future and he could not take four steps in the dark on another person's word, because for four steps the safety of Iwao would be in hands that were not Iwao's, and every cell in him had spent eleven years learning that his were the only hands that could be trusted not to drop the world.

18:11 — Three Steps

She did not get bored and she did not get cruel — which Ganryū would have, which most people would have. She just reset, and reset, her voice going flatter and steadier each time until it stopped being a test and became a metronome, a thing he could lean on precisely because it was so boringly, reliably there. The sun moved across the cliff-faces and turned them the color of a banked fire. His shoulders came down off his ears by degrees he didn't feel. And on the forty-somethingth try he closed his eyes and took three steps blind and did not flinch and did not look, and her hand caught his sleeve — stop, edge — and he stopped, heart going like a struck drum, having for three whole steps let another person hold the world. It was the smallest thing. A hairline crack in a wall a mountain had been building since he was old enough to be afraid. He would not have traded it for the leaf or the wind. None of those had ever cost him anything.

19:30 — Dinner

"Tomorrow we do five," Akemi had said, like it was nothing, and she had said we, and he turned the word over the whole walk home and all through his mother's stew. Gennai came in grey, washed to the elbow, and asked — he always asked it the same flat way — "Did you learn anything?" And Iwao, who had cut nothing and thrown nothing and molded nothing of note, said, "I let someone else lead." His father chewed on that a while. "Good," he said finally. "That one's heavier than the bloodline. Carry it anyway."

21:00 — The Arithmetic, Revised

By lamplight he did not study Dust. He set the grey chip back on the windowsill and sat with the arithmetic he had carried his whole life — if I hold everything, nothing falls — and found that the day had changed it by exactly one line. But I will be alone, and alone is also a kind of falling, just slower. He didn't have the line after that yet. He suspected it would take years to find, like the particle frame, like everything that was actually worth knowing.

22:34 — Lights Out

Mai's small weight against his side, the house ticking as it cooled. Above the rooftops the old man watched the heir's window go dark, and a quiet word climbed the chain that the Stoneward's heir had asked a classmate for help today, of all the words a prodigy could say. The village, which measured him by the leaf and the wind and the blood, would never understand why that was the most important thing he had done all week. Two people did. One was a sensor girl who had run the same drill forty times without once complaining. The other lay in the dark with his eyes open, learning to count to three.
