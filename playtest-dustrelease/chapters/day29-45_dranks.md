TITLE: Days 29–45 — The Work of Genin
DATES: Hidden Tor · Days 29 through 45 · D-ranks and the forging of a team
---
Nobody tells you that becoming a shinobi mostly means doing chores with your life on the line. Iwao learned it one rockslide, one runaway goat-herd, one tedious caravan escort at a time, in the company of a rival, an anchor, and a sensei who would have happily fed all three of them to the mountain if it made them sharper.

## Kurikara's Dawns
She had not been joking about dawn. Team Kurikara met at the cliff's edge while the village was still grey, and she ran them until the gift meant nothing — because, she said, on the day your chakra runs out and your clever blood can't save you, the only thing left is whether your legs keep moving and whether the people beside you trust you to keep moving with them. She drilled them as a unit until they stopped being three soldiers and started being one animal with three sets of eyes. She singled Iwao out for the cruelest of it, not despite the bloodline but because of it: *the strong ones learn to lean last, and they're the ones who get their teammates killed learning it.*

He leaned. By the fifteenth dawn he could fight back-to-back with Ganryū without watching him, trusting the broad boy's earth-walls to be where Ganryū said they'd be. He could move on Akemi's sensing in a melee the way he'd once moved on it blindfolded in a cistern lot. He was becoming, slowly, the thing Kurikara was forging: not a cannon, not even a prodigy. A *teammate* who happened to carry a legend.

## The Rival at His Back
The strangest gift of those weeks was Ganryū. Put on the same side of a fight, the year of resentment burned off like morning fog and left the thing underneath, which turned out to be respect with its sleeves rolled up. They competed at everything — who cleared more stone, who held a stance longer, who carried the heavier load up the switchback — and the competing made them both better, and neither would ever say out loud that it had become a kind of friendship. Ganryū still called him *heir,* but the way you'd call a brother by an old nickname. Iwao called him nothing, and watched his back, which between boys of the Hidden Tor meant more than any word.

## The Things the Body Learns
Mission Ryo went into a tin on the windowsill beside the grey chips. Iwao grew — sturdier, deeper-welled, his three natures coming cleaner and cheaper as Kurikara taught him to mold without waste. He did not reach for the bloodline. *Hold them like a frame, not a fist*, and he was still learning what a frame even felt like, in the small things, in the trusting. But he could feel the lesson moving through him sideways, the way water moves through stone — slowly, and then all at once, in some season you don't choose.

## The Road That Wasn't Routine (Day 45)
On the forty-fifth day the desk handed Team Kurikara another caravan escort along the eastern road — the same road the merchants had been muttering about, the bandits the first brief hadn't mentioned. Kurikara took the scroll, read the route, and went very still in the way Iwao had learned to read as *danger.* "Three carts. Two weeks of merchants too scared to use this road." She rolled the scroll shut. "That's not bandits. Bandits rob one cart and run. Somebody's holding the road." She looked at her three genin, weighing them against a thing she could not yet name. "Stay close. Do what I say the instant I say it. And Iwao —" the scarred jōnin held his eyes "— if it goes bad, you protect *them.* Not the carts. Not the village's opinion of you. *Them.*"

They set out at dawn, into a morning that smelled like rain and something underneath the rain, and the easy part of being a genin came quietly, unremarked, to its end.
