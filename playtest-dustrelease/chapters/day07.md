TITLE: Day 7 — The Seventh Day
DATES: Academy, Year One · Day 7 · the rest day, hour by hour
---
Six days the Hidden Stone drove its children up the cliff-stairs, and the seventh it pretended to let them rest, which in a village of shinobi meant only that the training moved indoors, into the family and the self, where no instructor could grade it.

## 07:12 — Clay
No bell, so he slept until the light, which felt like a crime and a gift at once. He found his mother already at the wheel and, for the first time in a while, sat down across from her and put his hands in the clay beside hers. Tsuru did not teach him so much as let him fail next to her — his walls collapsed, his rims went lopsided — and she said, watching a third pot slump, "You mold chakra like a master and clay like a goat." It was the first time she had teased him in a week, and he understood it was her way of saying she was less afraid today. The clay did not care that his hands were a legend. The clay only cared whether he was patient. He was not, yet. He kept the goat-pot anyway.

## 08:30 — The Errand
She sent him to the market for glaze-ash and a measure of river-iron, and the village taught him its other lesson, the one no class would. A vendor pressed extra into his basket and would take no coin — *for the heir, please, it's an honor* — and an old woman touched his sleeve as if for luck, and two children dared each other to ask if he could really turn a man to dust with a look, and he did not know where to put his face. He was learning that to be recognized is to be slightly *less* of a person each time — to become a story other people are telling, harder and harder to hear yourself think under. He gave the extra glaze-ash to the next stall that looked lean. It was all he could think to do with the weight of being looked at.

## 10:00 — Five Steps
He had promised Akemi five. They found an empty lot behind the cistern and she ran the blind walk, her voice the flat steady metronome it had become, and today he did not flinch at the first call, or the second. *Five steps. Stop. Turn. Three more.* He moved through the dark on her word like a boy wading into cold water he had decided, at last, to trust would hold him — and he made the five, and then made eight, and at the end stood with his eyes shut and his heart steady, which was the part that astonished him. The fear had not vanished. He had simply stopped letting it drive. "Tomorrow, ten," Akemi said, and he laughed, and the laugh surprised them both.

## 12:30 — The Table at the Stalls
Lunch with the misfit handful that had, without anyone deciding it, become *his* — Akemi loud, Tobari silent and watchful, even Suzume drifting over to sit at the edge with her perpetual kunai. Nobody talked about the bloodline. They talked about a jōnin's ridiculous haircut and whether the cistern-lot was haunted and whose mother made the best stewed eel. Iwao mostly listened, and found that listening to ordinary things spoken by people who were not afraid of him was its own kind of resting, deeper than sleep.

## 14:00 — The Frame, Again
An hour with his notes in the cool of the house. The particle frame, sketched and re-sketched — three forces in a tension so perfect the whole thing held still. He had begun to see it everywhere now: in the supply lines of the Second Tsuchikage's campaigns, in his mother's three-fingered grip on a rising pot, in the way Akemi's voice and his blind feet and the unseen ground had to balance for five steps to become eight. *Everything that holds, holds because three things pull against each other and none of them wins.* He didn't reach for the fusion. He just learned its shape a little better, the way you learn a face you are not yet allowed to meet.

## 16:00 — Two Boys, One Ground
He went to the training ground because he could not *not* train, even on the day for not training, and found Ganryū already there, alone, grinding through a kata for the hundredth time with his knuckles taped. They did not speak for an hour. They drilled near each other in a prickly, watchful silence — the gifted boy doing his monkish small-nature repetitions, the worked boy doing his thousand-rep forms — and something passed between them that was not friendship and was no longer quite hatred. At the end Ganryū wiped his face and said, not quite to him, "Didn't think you'd be here. Rest day." And Iwao said, "You're always here. Figured I'd see what you see in it." Ganryū looked at him a long moment, recalibrating a story he had been telling himself since the leaf fell into four pieces — *handed everything, earns nothing* — against the evidence of a prodigy who would not stop working. He didn't say anything else. He didn't have to. The shape of the rivalry had changed.

## 18:20 — The Quarry
On the way home he climbed to the lip of the great quarry where his father worked, and watched the evening shift come up out of the pit grey to the eyebrows. Gennai was down there somewhere in the dust that had killed his own father — Iwao's grandfather, a foreman who had breathed stone for forty years until his lungs turned to the very thing he cut. The family had given the Hidden Stone its walls for three generations, a fistful of grey lung at a time. And now the mountain had given the family a boy whose gift was to turn stone, cleanly and painlessly and completely, *to dust.* Iwao stood on the lip in the failing light and felt the awful symmetry of it: his whole line had died slowly of the dust, and he had been born able to *make* it, and he did not know whether that was a curse the mountain had finally paid out or a debt it was asking him to carry. He went down and walked his father home.

## 19:30 — Dinner, and the Grandfather
Over the stew Gennai talked about his own father for the first time Iwao could remember — the foreman's flat jokes, his refusal to wear the mask that might have saved him, his belief that a man who cut the mountain owed it the honesty of breathing it. "He'd have hated being proud of you," Gennai said, and then, after a while, "He'd have been proud of you anyway. He'd have just been gruff about it." Tsuru reached over and squeezed her husband's grey-cracked hand, and Mai fell asleep face-down in her rice, and Iwao understood that he was sitting at the exact center of the particle frame of his own family — three people in perfect tension, holding still, holding him.

## 21:15 — The Arithmetic, One More Line
By lamplight he set the grey chip back on the windowsill where it lived, and added a line to the arithmetic he had carried his whole life. *If I hold everything, nothing falls. But I will be alone. And alone is also falling. So: hold what is mine to hold, and let the rest be held by the people pulling against me — that is what makes a frame instead of a fist.* He suspected it was wrong, or at least incomplete, the way his first reach for the bloodline had been wrong. But it was a better wrong than the one he'd started the week with.

## 22:40 — The Watch That Does Not Rest
He slept. On the highest terrace the old man stood as he stood every night, and tonight a masked figure knelt at the rail beside him and reported, low, the small inventory of the heir's seventh day — the clay, the market, the blind walk, the quarry. Ōnoki listened to all of it without expression. "He went to the quarry," the Tsuchikage said at last, as if that were the only line that mattered, and perhaps to him it was. "He's looking for the cost." A long silence over the sleeping village. "Good. The ones who never look for the cost are the ones who make everyone else pay it." He waved the shadow away and stood a while longer alone, a small old man and a great dark mountain, watching one window, doing his own arithmetic about a boy and a bloodline and the years that were coming whether any of them were ready or not.
