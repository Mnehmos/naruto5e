TITLE: Act II, Chapter 6 — The Report Nobody Wants
DATES: Iwagakure · Day 95 · a boy decides who to hand a war to
---
There is a kind of knowledge that weighs more than any stone the quarry ever cut, and Iwao carried a piece of it home in a closed fist and did not sleep. A Hidden-Cloud blade-token, planted in the mud to be found. Proof of a lie shaped like proof of a war. Whoever he showed it to, the showing would decide something far above a Genin's pay grade — and he was, the whole grey morning, painfully aware that the village had a dozen hands reaching for exactly this, and that most of them would use it wrong.

## The Weight You Choose Where to Set Down
He could take it to Kazan, who would file it into quiet — *duplicates and clean copies* — and the truth would drown in the same kindness that drowned the road reports. He could take it to Captain Doki, who would hold it over his head like a torch and shout *Kumo!* until the shouting started a fire the token had been forged to light. He could take it to the mission hall and lay it on the official record, where it would stop being evidence and start being a *casus belli* the moment a clerk copied it down.

Or he could take it to the one person who had never once let him turn a thing he'd found into a thing he owned.

He took it to Kurikara.

## The One Who Won't Let Him Build a Throne
He found her at the training ground in the early light and opened his hand without preamble and laid the whole shape of it out — the bend's geometry, the recopied ledgers, the wagon they'd died for, the too-clean token dropped exactly where a grieving village would look. *It's a frame,* he said. *Someone wants us to blame the Cloud. I didn't want it on the record until you'd seen it.*

Kurikara looked at the token a long moment, and then at her student, and what she said he would carry as long as he'd carried his father's stone:

> "You finally brought something to the one person who won't let you turn it into a throne. Good." Her voice dropped, hard and low. "Now listen. If this is bait, you do not chase the hand that threw it. You show me the line, the lie, and who benefits — and *then* we decide who else gets to see it." Something in the scarred face that was almost, not quite, pride. "You did one thing right, heir. You brought it here before you tried to own the village with it."

It was the difference between a year ago and now, laid bare in a sentence. A year ago he'd have clenched the truth in his fist the way he clenched everything and tried to be the one who solved it. Today he'd opened his hand and given it to someone he trusted to pull the other end of the frame. *Trust, not control.* The cavern's lesson, the cell's lesson, the dust's lesson — the same lesson, again, in the shape of a forged token.

## The Report Nobody Wants
They could not keep it off the table entirely; a caravan had been hit, men were cut, a wagon was lost, and the eastern country had to be discussed. So at midday the mission hall filled with the people who decide things, and Iwao — a Genin, present only because the eyes that found it were his — tried to make a roomful of frightened adults *understand.*

He failed.

[OOC · Persuasion — d20 12, −1 (CHA 8, untrained) = 11 vs DC 15 · FAILURE. He cannot sway the room by force of personality; he is no orator, and a Genin's voice does not carry over captains and clerks.]

He was no orator — he never had been, the boy whose hands could split a leaf four ways and whose tongue could not talk his way out of a corner. His careful words about forgery and frame fell into a room that had already decided what it wanted to hear. Captain Doki seized on the attack as the proof of the threat he'd been screaming about for months — *I TOLD you, send men, treat the road as a killing ground* — and the more fiercely the captain wielded the heir's findings, the more the rest of the hall heard *Doki, again,* and discounted the whole of it. Kazan, from the other side, smoothed: *one caravan, one bad bend, let us not leap to wars over a road-clerk's tally and a boy's nerves.* Between the man who wanted it to mean war and the man who wanted it to mean nothing, the truth had no chair at the table, and a Genin could not give it one.

But Iwao had not come to *win* the room. He'd come to put the facts where they could not be unsaid. So while the captains argued, he laid it out — not pleaded, *laid* — the cold geometry, the recopied counts, the single wagon, the token too clean to be real, the whole irrefutable architecture of the lie, in the flat scholar's voice that had recited dead men's deeds since he was eleven.

[OOC · Ninshou (knowledge / insight) — d20 17, +7 = 24 vs DC 15 · SUCCESS (big). The logical case is airtight — undeniable to any mind actually weighing facts, even as the room argues past it.]

He could not make them *feel* it. He made it *true* on the record, in a form no one could later pretend hadn't been laid before them. The report came out of that hall the way the worst reports do — with no consensus, no decision, every faction reading the same facts into a different war or a different silence. The report nobody wanted. But the case now existed, entire and undeniable, waiting for one mind in the village clear enough to read it without first deciding what it should say.

That mind was on the terrace.

## The Verdict of the Small Old Man
Ōnoki had been measuring the heir since the first night — *what is the boy for* — and somewhere in the last six days the measuring had quietly finished. He summoned Iwao to the high terrace at the grey end of the afternoon, and he did not waste words.

He had read the case. Of course he had read it; he read everything that mattered, the way the boy did. He saw the frame for what it was in the time it took most men to find their spectacles. And he had watched, through a hundred small reports, what the heir had *done* with six ordinary days: protected children without performing, found a war in a book, survived being right, held a frame instead of crushing a man, and — the thing that decided it — carried the most dangerous object in the eastern country to his sensei instead of to the throne it could have built him.

"You were handed paper," the Third Tsuchikage said, looking out at the tightening east, "and you found a war in it, and you did not start it. Most men your age — most men *my* age — would have started it, for the glory of being the one who saw." He folded his hands on the cane. "I set you near responsibility to see if it would isolate you or swallow you. It did neither. You stayed a boy who trusts the people beside him, and became a shinobi the village can hand a hard thing to." A long pause, stone settling on stone. "So I am done treating you as a weapon to be kept in a drawer, or a child to be walked around the dangerous rooms. There is a hand behind this token, and it is patient, and it will try again in a shape you cannot simply pick up out of the mud. I am giving you leave — and the standing — to help find it. Not to fight it. To *find* it. Eyes before blood." The faintest dry almost-smile. "You have the eyes. Try not to be a legend about it."

Iwao walked down off the terrace into the dusk a different thing than he'd walked up — still a Genin, still unpromoted, still set to carry paper and walk children and assist his betters. But the village had, very quietly, decided to trust his eyes with its safety, and he understood that this was a heavier stone than the vest he hadn't won, and the right one, and that he would carry it the way his father carried stone and his mother carried clay: without crushing what walked beside him.

## What Waits in the East
Far down the dark roads, the patient man took the news of his failed clean war without heat. The heir had survived, the token had been *seen,* the frame had not taken — this time. Gomei the Hollow Road did not believe in luck or in heroes; he believed in patience and in the truth that any peace, given enough quiet pressure on the right seam, will tear itself. He had wanted a war he could hand the village. Now he would build one the village could not hand back. He began, without hurry, to plan a second shape — one with no token to find, no bend to read, no honest boy standing in the road. The kind of incident that does not need to be discovered to do its work.

And the Cloud envoy Raido, who had come to measure a bloodline and found himself measured into a frame he hadn't built and couldn't yet see, sent a careful letter home that said, in the language of careful men: *something is moving on the Stone's eastern border that is not the Stone, and not us, and it is using both our names.*

The mountain settled, certain it would outlast anything that climbed it. Below it a boy who could not charm a room or read a trail had been handed the village's eyes — because he was right, and careful, and could not be owned. The east tightened by one more silent turn. And somewhere a patient man began again.

*— End of the opening arc. The associate has become the eyes. The war that was almost handed over is only postponed. The climb goes on.*
