TITLE: Act II, Chapter 4 — Road Work
DATES: Hidden Tor, the eastern road · Day 93 · the ground starts talking, in a language he half-speaks

They sent him east to fix a road, which is how the village says *go look at the thing we are afraid of without admitting we are afraid.*

## A Day's March Out
The work-detail was four laborers, two genin, and a cart of tools, and Iwao, who was there to lift heavy things and be reassuring. The eastern road ran down off the mountain's grit into a drier, older country of switchbacks and blind bends and long sightlines that belonged to no one. A washout had taken a stretch of it — the spring-melt undercutting the old stonework until a wagon-length of road simply slumped into the gully. Routine. The kind of thing a road-detail patches a dozen times a season.

He had not been this far from the wall since the exam. The country felt different now that he knew how to listen to it — though, as the day would teach him, knowing you should listen and being *able* to is not the same gift.

## The Wrongness He Couldn't Read
He felt it before noon: a wrongness in the slumped ground, a sense that the water had not gone where water wants to go. He crouched over the washout and tried to read it the way a tracker would — was the watercourse moved? were the marker-stones shifted? had someone *helped* this road fall? — and the land would not answer him.

[OOC · Nature — d20 5, +7 = 12 vs DC 15 · FAILURE. He can't read engineered from natural; the woodsman's eye simply isn't his.]

He was a boy of stone halls and scrolls, not of wild country. He could feel that the ground was wrong the way you feel a wrong note without naming it, but the specific testimony of moved earth and redirected water stayed mute to him. A real tracker — Akemi, a hunting-nin, an old border scout — would have read the washout like a page. Iwao read it like a foreign language he'd only studied: he knew there were words there. He couldn't make them out. It was, in its quiet way, the most honest humbling the road could have given him — a reminder that the famous hands were not good at everything, that some gifts are earned in mud and not in libraries.

## What the Histories Said
But Iwao had one gift the mud couldn't take, and the bend gave him the chance to use it.

He stopped trying to read the *ground* and started reading the *shape.* He stood back, the way you step back from a painting, and looked at what the washout had done to the road — where it forced a wagon to slow, where the high side rose into cover, where the low side fell into a gully a man could lie in, where the only path around put your back to the slope and your flank to the trees. And the shape rang a bell struck eleven years deep, in the one classroom subject he had always loved.

[OOC · History — d20 16, +7 = 23 vs DC 14 · SUCCESS (big). He recognizes the reshaped bend as textbook staged-ambush geometry from the battle-histories.]

He had read this. Not this road — this *shape.* In the chronicle of the Border Skirmishes, in the dry diagrams of a campaign sixty years gone, in the margins he'd filled as a boy reciting the deeds of dead shinobi: the killing-bend, the engineered chokepoint, the place you *make* a road become so that the road does your killing for you. The washout hadn't just slumped. Whether by water or by hand he could not prove — but the bend it left behind was not a wound. It was a *blueprint.* Someone, somewhere, was either building an ambush ground here or had built one exactly like it before and the land remembered. He could not tell you how the stones were moved. He could tell you what they had been moved *into,* because he had loved the right book.

A boy who read had found the war before the trackers did. The trouble with that, he was about to learn, is that nobody sends men to a battlefield on the say-so of a boy and a book.

## The Garrison, and the Captain Who Cried for Men
At dusk they came to the eastern garrison post — a tired wooden thing manned by tired proud soldiers — and Iwao reported the bend to its captain, plainly, with the careful hedge of an honest scholar: *I can't prove the washout was deliberate, but the bend matches staged-ambush geometry from the histories.*

Captain Doki heard it like a man hearing rain after a drought.

> "Then we stop treating this as a slip of weather and start treating it as a killing ground." His voice went hard and bright at once. "I want the heir's report copied to my hand, the washout marked on the eastern map, and a rider sent at once to Hidden Tor: the road matches staged ambush geometry from the old battle-histories. If they want proof —" and here something desperate showed through the iron "— they can send men to count the corpses before they happen."

[OOC · Captain Doki (eastern garrison, npc_2486c7f4) — on-demand npc_turn → social_speak. His standing goal: win reinforcements by inflating the danger he also genuinely believes in.]

And Iwao, who had carried the warning in good faith, watched it become something else in the captain's hands — a *weapon in an old argument.* Doki had been begging Hidden Tor for men for months; everyone knew it; the command read his alarms the way you read the boy who cried wolf. Now the captain had the famous heir's name to wave, and he would wave it hard, and the very force of his waving would make the truth easier to dismiss. *Doki again. Doki and his killing grounds. Doki using a Genin's book-report to pry loose a garrison.* Iwao could see the shape of that, too — another blueprint, this one made of politics — and it frightened him more than the bend had. The truest warning he could give was about to be discounted precisely because a desperate man believed it.

He rode back toward the wall in the dark with two cold certainties: that something was being built on the eastern road, and that the village had three good reasons not to believe it yet.

## The Board, the Next Morning
Far down the dark road behind him, the patient man had already moved on. The reshaped bend had served its purpose — a rehearsal, a casting of the geometry, a thing left where a scout or a sharp-eyed boy might find it and cry alarm and be disbelieved, softening the village's trust in its own eastern reports. Gomei did not need *that* bend. He needed a fat caravan on a bigger road with no heir standing on it. And the syndicate, bleeding wagons and too proud to say so, was about to send exactly that.

By the time Iwao reached the mission desk the next morning, the board had a new slip pinned to it, edged in the orange of a C-rank: *caravan escort, eastern supply line, departs at dawn.* And a tired dam-keeper named Kazan, who had finally started writing things down, looked at the boy who kept finding the things the village was trying not to see, and made a decision that would put the heir on a road after all.
