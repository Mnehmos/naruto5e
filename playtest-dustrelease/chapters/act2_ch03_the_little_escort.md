TITLE: Act II, Chapter 3 — The Little Escort
DATES: Iwagakure · Day 92 · the smallest duty, and the first deliberate hand
---
The board gave him children.

Not as an insult — though it could have been read as one, and Ganryū, two slots down the duty roster, plainly read it as one. The mission desk needed a handful of Academy first-years walked from the inner village to the outer training ground, the safe road, the morning patrol's own beat. A D-rank a year below his year. *Walk the babies to the field.* Kazan slid him the slip without looking up, and Iwao understood it was the same test as the paper-carrying: here is the lightest possible stone; show me whether you sulk under it.

He didn't sulk. He'd lost a tournament eleven days ago for clenching at the weight of being alone; he was not going to clench at the weight of being small.

## The Children
There were six of them, and one of them was Kosuke.

The boy went stiff when he saw who'd be walking them — the dust-heir, the legend, the one who'd knelt down in the classroom and shown him his scarred hands. For a moment Iwao watched the old worship war with the new, more complicated thing he'd planted on Day 90. Then Kosuke fell in at his elbow, and after a hundred paces of stony eight-year-old silence, opened his fist — literally, held up his hand and spread the fingers, shy about it — and said, "I've been practicing. The loose way. It's harder." Iwao said, "It's supposed to be," and did not make it a lesson, did not make it about himself, just walked, and let the boy walk a little taller beside him. *That* was the whole job. He was starting to believe it.

The road was the safe one. Stone underfoot, the village wall at their backs, the morning thin and clear. Nothing on this road had gone wrong in years.

## The Stumble That Was Too Clean
At the blind bend — there is always a blind bend — a cart had lost a wheel.

It was a good cart and a bad accident. The wheel lay too neatly. The carter was too unbothered, and too interested; he made the small talk of a man reading from a list, and the questions under the small talk were all the wrong shape: *busy road today? the patrol come by regular? what time do they usually — ah, never mind an old man's chatter.* A second man loitered at the treeline edge, doing the specific nothing of someone holding a count in his head.

Iwao felt the wrongness before he could name it, the way he'd felt the skipped rhythm in the ledgers — and this time he looked, and named it.

Not bandits. Not yet. *Measurers.* Someone wanted to know how fast the Hidden Stone moved when its little ones were on the open road, and how, and from where — the exact data you gather before you turn a road into an incident. The cart was a question. The children were the variable.

A year ago — a tournament ago — Iwao might have answered the question loudly. Unmade the cart. Pinned the carter. Shown the watching count exactly how fast and how terrible the heir could be, and handed them, for free, the very reading they'd come for.

He didn't. He held the frame.

He moved the children to his off-side without hurrying, put the stone wall of his own unbothered body between them and the bend, told Kosuke a flat joke so the little ones would laugh and keep walking, and went *past* the broken cart at an ordinary pace, giving the carter an ordinary nod and the loiterer nothing at all — no jutsu, no threat, no reading. He gave the measurers a blank page. And he marked them: the carter's hands (uncalloused, wrong for a carter), the loiterer's sightline, the minutes, the direction the count would walk when it left.

## The Treeline Empties
Behind him, in the green dark past the bend, the patient man who had sent the cart watched his clean little probe come back dirty.

He had wanted a number — *response time, children on road* — and instead he had a problem: the heir had looked at his stopwatch and declined to wind it. Worse, the heir had not panicked, had not performed, had simply *removed the variable and walked on,* which told Gomei more than any jutsu would have. He pulled his men back into the trees before the count could be made, and revised his patience upward, and let the village keep its little road one more day. The incident he was building did not need this road. It needed a bigger one, with no heir on it. He went to find that road.

## The Quiet Word
At the desk that evening Iwao did the small brave thing the day had been shaped, all along, to ask of him: he made Kazan write it down.

Not loudly. Not across the room. He laid it out plain — a staged mishap at the outer bend, a man dressed as a carter who was not one, a second man counting, questions aimed at patrol timing with children on the road — and he asked, in the language Kazan respected, for it to be *logged,* properly, through the proper channel the man was always invoking. Kazan looked at him a long moment, the tired dam-keeper weighing one more cup of water. Then he wrote it down. It was, Iwao would only later understand, the first report about the eastern country that did not get smoothed — because the one who carried it had eyes the village had already decided to trust before it decided to trust his blood.

Up on the terrace the old man heard that the heir had been handed children and a baited bend, and had protected the one and refused the other, and had come home and made the desk *remember.* Ōnoki almost smiled. *He gave them a blank page,* the Tsuchikage thought. *Good. The dangerous ones always want to be read. This one is learning to be unreadable.* Then, because he was a Kage and slept little: *And someone is reading our roads. Find out who.*

The eastern country tightened by one more turn, very quietly, the way a mountain settles. And for the first time it was tightening around a thing the village could no longer pretend it hadn't been told.
