TITLE: Days 47–58 — The Exhibition
DATES: Hidden Tor · Days 47 through 58 · the Particle art shown to the world
---
A village does not keep a thing like the Particle art a secret. It cannot. Secrets that large leak through stone. So when the Stoneward decided the time had come, he did not hide the heir — he *displayed* him, which is a more dangerous and more honest thing.

## Refining (Days 47–55)
First, the work. Vohl came down from his terrace more than once in those weeks — an event that made jōnin go pale — and taught the boy himself, in the sealed cavern, the difference between a flicker and a technique. *Hold the frame. Now make it smaller. Now make it last.* Under that ancient, exacting eye Iwao's Particle steadied from the wild grey of his first reach into something disciplined enough to show in daylight: **Particle Release: Honed Detachment**, the second rung of a ladder the old man swore would take a lifetime to climb. Iwao did not rush it. He had learned, finally, that the bloodline rewarded patience and punished hunger, and he had run enough cliff-stairs to know the difference in his legs.

## The Exhibition (Day 56)
The Hidden Tor threw open its lower terraces and let the village — and the foreign dignitaries the season's trade had brought through the gates — watch its genin teams demonstrate what a year of grinding makes. Team Kurikara moved like the single animal she'd forged them into; Ganryū's earth-walls rose where Iwao didn't have to look, Akemi's sensing threaded them through a mock-ambush blind, and the crowd, which had come to gawk at the heir, found itself watching a *team.*

And then, at the end, on a stone effigy raised for the purpose, Iwao showed them the Particle art.

He held the frame the way Vohl had taught him — open-handed, gripping nothing — and the Honed Detachment bloomed clean and controlled and *aimed,* and the effigy's heart simply ceased to be, a perfect cored hollow drifting away as grey nothing while the rest of the figure stood untouched. *Control.* Not the army-grinding horror of the legend, but a scalpel. The village exhaled as one. A grandmother wept. Somewhere a child whispered Vohl, the Third Stoneward's name and, for the first time in Iwao's life, it did not feel like a weight pressing him flat. It felt like a torch being handed forward.

## The Eyes from Other Villages
But the foreign eyes in that crowd did not weep. They *calculated.* A genin of the Hidden Tor had just demonstrated **Particle Release** — the the Particle art, the particle bloodline thought to have died with its last wielder's age — alive again, in a child, controlled. By nightfall fast birds were leaving Hidden Tor for capitals that would very much like to know how a dead bloodline had come back, and whether it could be bought, stolen, copied, or killed before it grew. Kurikara watched the birds go from the cliff and said nothing, but she added a third lock to the team's training-ground gate the next morning, and started teaching them, without explaining why, how to spot a tail.

## The Entry
On the fifty-eighth day the scroll came down from the mountain with the Stoneward's seal: **Team Kurikara is entered in the Chūnin Selection Exam.** The other villages would send their best genin to the joint exam, as they always did — part tournament, part diplomacy, part the only sanctioned place the Five Nations got to measure each other's young blades short of war. And Hidden Tor would send, among others, the boy with the reborn dust, precisely *because* the other villages were already asking about him.

"They'll all be watching you," Kurikara told the three of them, flat as ever. "Some of them will want to beat you. A few of them will want you dead before you're grown. Good." She rolled the scroll shut. "Better you learn what that feels like in an exam, where the proctors are obligated to stop the killing, than on a battlefield, where they are obligated to start it. We leave for the exam in three days. Iwao —" the old look, the one with no awe in it "— you are not a demonstration anymore. Out there, the Particle art is a target painted on all three of our backs. Carry it like it's ours, not yours."

He understood. He pocketed the small grey chip his father had given him for the field, and went home to tell his mother he was leaving, and did not tell her the part about the foreign birds.
