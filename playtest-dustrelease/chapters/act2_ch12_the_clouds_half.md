TITLE: Act II, Chapter 12 — The Cloud's Half
DATES: Hidden Tor · Day 101 · two villages' buried shames laid on one table, and a boy who decides to walk into the kind of place that made the man he hunts
---
There is a thing an Tor shinobi is taught from the cradle never to do, and Iwao did it on purpose, in a quiet room, to an enemy.

He had the wound's outline now — the Stone's grey half of it, the Eleven of the Hollow Pass, the engagement scrubbed and the accord signed clean. But the Stone's ledgers would only ever hold the Stone's share of the lie, and to know the whole shape of the man he hunted he needed the other half, which lived in records no boy of the Hidden Tor would ever be allowed to read. There was exactly one person in reach who might hold it, and he was a loyal envoy of the Cloud, and asking him to confirm his own village's complicity in murdering its own dead was the sort of request that gets a man recalled, or worse.

So Iwao did not ask. He confessed first.

## The Table, and What He Put On It
He met Raido in a borrowed room with the shutters half-drawn and laid the Stone's ledger open on the table between them before a single question was asked — the scrubbed engagement, the eleven Cloud names that lived in one roster and then nowhere, the suspiciously clean date on the border accord. *This is what my village did,* the open page said, in the Stone's own tired hand. *I am showing it to you before I ask you for anything. I am giving you my home's shame for free.*

Raido looked at it a long time. Then he looked at the boy who had set it there, and something in the Cloud envoy's measured face shifted, the way a stone shifts when the frost finally gets under it.

> "You honor me with a confession before a demand. That is a rarer courtesy than most treaties." He drew the ledger toward him with two fingers, gently, the way you handle something that has bones in it. "Let me look upon it with you, and I will answer as plainly as I am able: if the Cloud erased those names, I will not pretend innocence for the comfort of either village. If Gomei was born from that wound, then we should measure it together — rather than let old men's silence keep the score. You have my thanks for speaking first."

And so two villages' buried shames met on one small table, and the picture they made together was worse and clearer than either half alone. The Cloud had not lost contact with that cell by accident. The Cloud command had *decided* to lose them — had let the Stone do the wiping and then disowned the dead to keep its own hands clean for the accord, struck their names from the Cloud's rolls the same week the Stone struck them from its logs. Eleven shinobi, murdered by one village and abandoned by their own, erased twice over so that two exhausted nations could call a thing peace and be praised for it. Raido's jaw was tight the whole time he read. He was a loyal man learning, in real time, the exact price his loyalty had once been built on.

"There was a twelfth," he said finally, low. "The Cloud's rolls have a ghost in them — a runner from a cell that 'never deployed,' a name that should not appear and appears anyway, once, struck through. And for eighteen years there have been border rumors the Cloud files under weather: a man who moves through the contested march like he owns its silences, who turns up wherever two villages are about to trust each other and leaves them not trusting. We never gave him a name. You did. The Hollow Road." He closed the ledger. "He has been living in the wound this whole time, heir. He never left it."

## Where An Erased Man Lives
That was the thread Iwao's mind had been reaching for, and now that Raido had set the Cloud's ghost beside the Stone's eleven, it pulled taut and the whole geometry snapped into place the way the killing-bend had, the way the chain of office had.

[OOC · Ninshou (predict where a man erased from BOTH villages' records would operate and go to ground, with both buried halves now joined) — d20 **12**, +7 = **19** vs DC 17 · SUCCESS. The pattern roll, his element: the lens reads a location out of two villages' silences.]

A man with no village does not hide in either country — both have eyes, both have records, both would eventually file him. He hides in the place that belongs to *neither*: the contested highlands around the Hollow Pass itself, the disputed march that the Stone and the Cloud have spent eighteen years agreeing not to officially see — because to see it is to remember what was bought there. It was perfect ground for the Hollow Road precisely *because* of the lie: a stretch of country both villages were institutionally blind to, patrolled by no one, claimed by everyone, recorded by neither. Gomei had not fled into hiding after the courtyard. He had simply gone home — to the only ground in the world that was as erased as he was.

To find him, Iwao would have to go to the Hollow Pass. He would have to leave Stone soil and walk into the exact no-man's-land that had made the man, into a march where, if it went wrong, the village could disown him as cleanly as both villages had once disowned eleven better shinobi than he was. He understood the shape of that, sitting there. He was proposing to walk willingly into the kind of place, and the kind of silence, that creates a Gomei.

He went to ask his cell anyway, because the whole point of everything he had learned was that he would not walk into it alone.

## The Cell That Crosses
He told them plainly in the dusk — Akemi, who had been his sensor and his conscience since the year he treated teammates as math problems; Ganryū, who had spent that same year deciding whether to surpass him or hate him and had landed, grudgingly, on the first. He told them it was off Stone soil, into neutral ground, out from under the village's protection, with a real chance of being disavowed. He did not dress it up. He had learned not to.

Ganryū snorted and said something about how someone had to keep the heir from walking off a cliff while reading, and that was a yes in the only dialect Ganryū spoke. But it was Akemi who answered the thing underneath the question — the old thing, the thing she had named in him when they were children and he was afraid of his own hands.

> "I'm coming with you." Quiet, the way she said the true things. "But don't make yourself a symbol for us to follow — make room for me to keep you human. If you freeze, I'll tell you. If you start carrying this alone, I'll stop you." A breath. "And if you're afraid of your own hands — then use mine."

[OOC · Akemi (npc_907075f3) — on-demand model turn → social_speak (whisper). The Day-5 blind-spot ("he holds everyone at arm's length because he fears his own hands") answered, years later, by the same friend who named it: use mine. The thesis of the whole story handed back to its hero as a gift.]

Iwao did not trust himself to answer that out loud, so he just nodded, and the nod held everything. The boy who had once solved problems by removing the variables was going to walk into the most dangerous silence on the map carrying three other people's hands on purpose, because the man he was hunting had been made by a silence that left him with none.

## The Sanction, and the Echo
Vohl gave the crossing his blessing and could not give it his protection — that was the whole grim arithmetic of the contested march, and the old man named it without flinching. Out there they would be Hidden Tor shinobi only as long as it was convenient for two villages to say so. Step wrong in the Hollow Pass and the Stone could lose them the way it had lost the Eleven, and call it weather.

"You understand what you're asking to do," Vohl said. It was not quite a question.

"I'm asking to go where eleven people were erased," Iwao said, "and not be erased, and not erase anyone. To stand in the exact place the lie was made and refuse to make another one." He paused. "He thinks the only honest thing left is a war. I want to prove there's a more honest thing than that, on the same ground where they proved there wasn't."

The Stoneward looked at his heir for a long moment with an expression that had buried too many shinobi to spend hope cheaply, and spent a little anyway. "Then go to the Hollow Pass, lens," he said. "And bring back more than you carry in. That's the only kind of crossing that's ever been worth the loss."

They would leave at dawn. East and down, off the mountain, into the country that keeps no ledger — toward a pass that two nations had agreed to forget, and one man had refused to.

*— He gave away his village's shame to earn the Cloud's, and the truth they made together pointed home to a wound: the Hollow Road never left the Hollow Pass. So that is where the lens will go — carrying hands, into the silence that makes men carry none.*
