TITLE: Act II, Chapter 7 — The Hand Behind the Token
DATES: Hidden Tor · Day 96 · trusting an enemy to catch a worse one

Vohl's charge was four words long and heavier than the vest Iwao hadn't won: *find the hand.* Not fight it. Find it. Eyes before blood. And so the heir of the Hidden Tor began the work he was actually built for — not the cutting of leaves four ways, but the reading of a lie back to the one who wrote it.

He started, of course, in the archives. He always started where the books were.

## The Wall the Books Hit
Kurikara worked it with him, which meant she sat with her arms folded and made him say his reasoning aloud so the holes showed. Where is a Hidden-Cloud blade-token forged? Who keeps the dies, the alloys, the script? If the planted token was false — and it was, too clean, too convenient — then someone had made a false one, and a made thing has a maker, and a maker has a hand, and a hand can be traced.

He laid the token under the archive lamp and brought everything he had to bear on it: the metallurgy texts, the diplomatic registers of Cloud marks, the long scholar's patience that had carried him through every test that lived on a page.

And for once the page failed him. Not because he wasn't sharp enough — because the answer simply wasn't *there.* Hidden Tor's archives could tell him a hundred things about Cloud tokens and not one of them was the thing he needed, because the forgery was good — *expert* good — made by someone who knew the Cloud's own hand better than any Stone book ever could. Iwao sat back from the lamp with the old, useful humbling settling over him for the third time that week: the bend he couldn't read, the room he couldn't sway, and now the forgery he couldn't trace. His gift had edges. The library had a far wall, and he'd just walked into it.

Kurikara watched him hit the wall and did not soften it. "So the book doesn't have it," she said. "Then stop asking the book. Who *would* have it? Who in all the world can look at a forged Cloud token and tell you, on sight, exactly what's wrong with it — and who's good enough to have forged it?" She let the answer arrive on its own. It arrived wearing Cloud grey.

## The Tea Left Unspilled
Raido found him that afternoon — or let himself be found, which with Raido was the same gesture performed from the other side. A quiet teahouse, neutral ground, two cups and a great deal left unsaid in the steam between them.

> "Your caution does you credit," the envoy said, and there was less measuring in him today and more of something tired and real. "In a place like this, the finest tea is not the kind poured, but the kind left unspilled." He turned his cup a quarter-turn on the table. "If you have time, I would hear what in the caravan scene struck you as wrong — and I will tell you, plainly and without insult, what details of a Cloud token could only be forged by someone familiar with our hand."

[OOC · Raido of the Cloud (npc_5251c1ab) — on-demand npc_turn → social_speak, his own declaration. The framed envoy offers the alliance; his lead is the thing the archive lacked.]

It was the cleanest thing anyone had said to Iwao in six days, because it was honest about the trade. *You show me your piece. I show you mine.* Raido had come to Hidden Tor to measure the heir's bloodline for the Cloud's quiet files, and Iwao knew it, and Raido knew he knew it — and none of that mattered now, because someone had decided to forge a war with both their names on it, and the envoy who'd come to spy had discovered he was being made the villain of a story he hadn't written. The man being blamed wanted the framer found at least as badly as the boy who'd nearly died on the staged road.

So Iwao did the hardest version of the only lesson he'd ever really been taught. He'd learned to trust the cell. He'd learned to trust the natures in his own hand. He'd learned to trust the right adult over the easy one. Now he learned the last, worst grade of it: he trusted an *enemy* — carefully, with his eyes open, the way you hold a frame and not a fist — because the enemy in front of him was the only one who could help him catch the worse one he couldn't see.

He told Raido what the caravan had screamed: the wagon they'd died for, the token dropped too clean, the whole architecture of a manufactured provocation. And Raido, true to the trade, told him the thing the archive couldn't:

The forgery was *intimate.* Not the work of some Stone craftsman copying a Cloud mark from a register, getting the script right and the soul wrong. Whoever made that token knew the Hidden Cloud's hand the way you know a language you were raised in — the private weight of the strokes, the alloy the registers never list, the small deliberate imperfections a real Cloud smith leaves and a forger never thinks to. *"This was not made by someone imitating us,"* Raido said quietly, and for the first time the pleasant mask was entirely gone. *"It was made by someone who is, or was, one of us."*

## What the Lead Cost
A Cloud-connected hand. A rogue who knew Cloud from the inside, forging Cloud's guilt to light a war between the Cloud and the Stone. The shape of the enemy narrowed by one terrible degree — and widened, too, because it meant the patient man on the eastern road either *was* a Cloud defector or *kept* one, and either way the thing being built was bigger and older and more deliberate than road-banditry. Someone with a grudge that crossed a border was using two villages as the blade and the throat.

Iwao walked home in the dark carrying a heavier hand than he'd left with — not the token now, but the *knowledge,* which was worse. He had trusted an enemy and the enemy had been straight with him, and that was its own kind of vertigo. His father, over the stone-pot, heard only the shape of it — *I'm working with someone I'm not supposed to trust, to stop something worse* — and set down his bowl with the old care.

"A frame needs more than one force pulling," Gennai said slowly, "or it isn't a frame. You taught me that, telling me about your dust." He looked at his son a long moment. "Just be sure, boy, that when you hold the other end of a rope with an enemy, you both know which of you is the one trying to start the fire — and that it's neither of you." He didn't smile. "Find the one it is."

Up on the terrace the old man heard that the heir had failed to trace the forgery from a book and had, instead of forcing it, gone and *borrowed the eyes of the very Cloud he was meant to suspect* — and had come back with a truth worth more than the whole archive: *the forger is one of theirs, gone rogue.* Vohl sat with that the way he sat with everything, certain and unsurprised. *He cannot read a trail or sway a room or crack a forgery,* the Stoneward thought, *and he found the one thread the whole village missed, by trusting the one man the whole village would have shut out. Eyes before blood. And the eyes know how to borrow other eyes.* He tapped the cane once. *The boy is not a weapon. He is becoming a lens. Good. We are going to need one.*

And to the east, the patient man — who had not been one of the Cloud for many years, and had never forgiven them for the making or the casting-out — heard nothing yet of the teahouse, and continued, without heat, to build the second shape: the incident that would need no token, no bend, no honest boy to find it. He had used the Cloud's hand once and been seen. Next time he would use something the Stone could not refuse to believe, because it would come from inside the Stone itself.
