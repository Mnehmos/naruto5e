TITLE: Day 3 — The Nature He Was Told Not to Touch
DATES: Academy, Year One · Day 3
---
Everything came free to Iwao except the one thing the whole village wanted from him. Today he would find the edge of that thing, and it would very nearly take a piece of him with it.

## The Seam
A visiting instructor opened the morning by dropping a genjutsu over the entire class — a layered illusion, soft and total, the lesson being to *find the seam.*

Iwao could not cast an illusion to save his life. Genjutsu was about *feeling*, and his gifts had never lived in his feelings; they lived in his hands and, it turned out, in his cold analyst's mind. While other children groped at phantoms, he sat very still and took the illusion apart from the inside, naming each place where one layer failed to meet the next, until the false world fell away from him like a shed skin. The instructor blinked at the boy who couldn't weave a single mirage but couldn't be *held* by one either.

It went into the ledger of him that he was keeping without meaning to: weak where it was about feeling, formidable where it was about understanding. A genjutsu-user might fool him once. Never twice.

## Behind the Shed
The terror of the day before had not made him want *less.* That was the thing no one understood about him. He had come a single breath from crushing a boy, and the lesson his body took from it was not *be careful* but *understand the thing inside you before it does that on its own.*

So at the noon break he went where no one would look — behind the equipment shed, in the thin shadow — folded the Ram, and reached. Past wind. Past fire. Past earth. For the place where all three were meant to fuse into the bloodline the whole village whispered about, the legend his own hands had been built to carry.

It did not come free.

The three natures lurched in his chest and *caught wrong*, grinding against one another like millstones with no grain between them. His fingertips went dead and cold all at once. And behind his shoulder a patch of the shed wall no bigger than a coin sighed quietly into grey powder and was simply — *gone.* Not broken. Not burned. *Unmade.* For one heartbeat Iwao tasted what the legend actually was, underneath the glory the village had wrapped around it, and it was not glory at all. It was *deletion.* And it did not care in the least whose hand it sat in.

This was the truth the whole story would turn on, and he found it alone, in the dirt, at eleven years old: his control came free. The bloodline would have to be *earned.* The one nature that mattered was the only thing that had ever refused his gifted hands.

## The White Face
He never heard the sensei come.

Ryūen saw the numb fingers. He saw the hole in the wall where solid stone had decided to stop existing. And the color went out of his face entirely.

The forbidding was absolute, and it came fast and hard — *never alone, never unsupervised, not for years, do you understand me, never again* — but under the anger was something Iwao had not expected, and it frightened him more than the wall had: it was *fear.* Old fear, carried a long time. This was a man who had seen, once, what half-formed Particle does to the body of a child who reached too soon, and had never been able to set the image down.

He marched Iwao back to the range by the collar and supervised an afternoon of deliberately, almost insultingly *small* work — wind held thin as thread, earth shaped into clean dull blocks, nothing fused, nothing reaching — and he did not look away from the boy for a single second until the last bell rang.

## The Swallowed Stone
Iwao told his mother nothing about the wall. He lay awake instead, turning the day over and over: that everything was easy except the only thing they wanted, and that the only thing they wanted could erase the people he loved by accident, between one breath and the next.

He decided, again, and harder than before, that he would not touch it until he could hold it *perfectly* — and he understood now that *perfectly* was very, very far away, and that the distance to it was the whole point.

Above the rooftops, the old man on the terrace watched the boy's window the way you watch a fire you are not yet sure you can control, and the village — which had not seen the wall, and would have only loved him more if it had — leaned one degree closer to the heir who lay in the dark being quietly, completely terrified of his own birthright.
