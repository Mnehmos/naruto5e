TITLE: Chapter 2 — Hands Like His Grandfather's
DATES: Academy, Year One · Days 8–14
---
There is a cruelty in being good at things you did not choose.

Iwao learned it on the shuriken range, where Kanna-sensei chalked a target the size of a coin onto the far wall and told the class that *hitting it once* by week's end would earn a mark of merit. Iwao hit it on his second throw. Then his third. Then he threw three at once and stitched them into the chalk in a tight triangle, and the range went very quiet. *(Dexterity check — natural 19, total 21 vs DC 13.)*

"Hands like his grandfather's," someone whispered, though no one in that yard had ever met the Third Tsuchikage, and Iwao's actual grandfather had been a quarry foreman who died of dust in his lungs. The village had a way of folding every gift back into the same legend. Iwao gathered his shuriken and said nothing, because what was there to say? *I practiced* would be a lie. He hadn't. The hands simply knew.

## The Spar

Ganryū had been waiting for this all week.

When Tetsuna-sensei paired them for the first sanctioned taijutsu spar — chalk circle, first to three solid touches or a yield — the broad boy cracked his knuckles and grinned the grin of someone who had finally been handed the thing he wanted. *Here*, the grin said. *Here is where the wind gives you nothing. Here is where I earned it and you didn't.*

The bell rang. *(Combat — initiative: Iwao 10, Ganryū 3. Iwao moves first.)*

And Iwao's body — those traitor hands, that uncanny economy — simply moved. He slipped inside Ganryū's reach and tapped him twice, clean, beneath the ribs. *(Round 1: Iwao hits, 20 vs AC 13, 5 damage.)* Ganryū roared and came in with a stone-reinforced rush that Iwao wasn't even sure he *dodged* so much as *wasn't standing where it landed.* *(Ganryū's Stone-Fist Rush — 11 vs AC 12, miss.)*

"Stop *flinching* and fight me!" Ganryū bellowed.

"I'm not—" Iwao started, and then Ganryū's Boulder Hook came around like the swing of a quarry crane, and Iwao's feet carried him under it, and his palm found Ganryū's sternum a third time, and the chalk circle was over. *(Round 2: Iwao hits, 22 vs AC 13, 4 damage → Ganryū 3/12. Ganryū's Boulder Hook — natural 1, fumble, wide.)*

"Point. Match." Tetsuna-sensei's voice did not rise. "Iwao."

The yard did not cheer. They never did, for him. They simply *looked* — the way you look at weather.

## What It Costs

Ganryū did not shake his hand. He stood in the broken chalk breathing hard, and when he finally spoke it was quiet, and it was worse than shouting.

"You don't even *want* it." He wiped his mouth. "I will be at that range before dawn and after dark every day until my hands bleed, and you will still be better, because something *handed* it to you." His eyes were wet and furious. "So I'll just have to work until *handed* isn't enough. Remember that, heir." *(Ganryū's disposition toward Iwao: −1; he resolves to out-work the bloodline.)*

He walked off. Akemi appeared at Iwao's shoulder, chewing her lip, her sensor's sense of the room written all over her round face. "He's not wrong that it's unfair," she said carefully. "He's wrong about *you*, though. You looked like you wanted to apologize the whole time." She bumped his arm. "That's not nothing either."

That night Iwao lay awake. *(Long rest; the Will of Fire renewed.)* On the highest terrace a small old man received a one-line report from a masked figure who was very good at not being seen — *the boy took the spar; took it gently* — and grunted, and adjusted the village's quiet ledger of the heir by a notch he did not explain. *(World tick: Ōnoki watches over Iwao; iwagakure standing → 9.)*

Iwao only knew that something had come too easily again, and that a boy he might have liked now hated him for it, and that the wind off the cliffs had no opinion at all. He pressed his gifted, useless-feeling hands flat against the cold stone of the wall, and for the first time wished — fiercely, childishly — that he could give some of it *away.*

He did not yet know that the thing the village most wanted from him was the one thing he had never once been able to make come easily. He did not yet know what it cost to wake the dust.

He would learn.
