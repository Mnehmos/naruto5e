TITLE: Day 1 — The Boy With the Quiet Hands
DATES: Academy, Year One · Day 1 · Iwagakure, the Hidden Stone
---
The Hidden Stone did not sleep so much as settle — slowly, with great weight, certain it would outlast anything that tried to climb it. Iwao had tasted its grit on his teeth every morning of his life. This was the first morning the mountain seemed to be waiting to see what he would do.

## Before the Bell
He woke on the floor-mat in the grey before dawn, to the smell of wet clay and the soft, endless turning of his mother's wheel. Tsuru was always up first; she shaped pots the way other people breathed, and she watched her son the way other people watched weather they weren't sure of.

Before anything else, Iwao did the thing she had taught him when he was small and frightened of his own hands. He sat. He breathed. He laced his fingers into the Ram seal and reached — past the warm and the cold of himself — until three threads rose into his palm at once: the brown of turned earth, the pale of moving air, the red of something that wanted to burn. He did not *do* anything with them. He only held them, steady as a held breath, so that they would never do anything he had not asked.

The threads did not so much as tremble. Behind him the potter's wheel slowed, just for a moment, and then went on. His mother said nothing. Her shoulders were very still.

Mai found him, of course. His little sister had a sensor's nose for exactly the thing she was supposed to sleep through, and she padded in demanding *the sparkly hands* until he wove a thread of light between his fingers and she shrieked with joy. Their father, Gennai, came through dressed for the quarry, hands cracked into a pale map of a life spent lifting heavy things. He cuffed Iwao's shoulder once on his way to the door.

"Hidden Stone made you," he said. "Don't let it spend you."

Then he was gone into the grit-light, and for one more morning the house was only a house.

## The Leaf
Tetsuna-sensei was built like a retaining wall and disapproved of erosion. She set a single dry leaf on every desk in the first-year room and folded her arms.

"Channel one nature and cut it. One clean cut. Most of you will mangle it."

The room filled with the small sounds of children trying. Iwao set three fingers against his leaf and did not think about being watched. He threaded a sliver of *wind*, then a sear of *fire*, then a fold of *earth*, each in its turn — and the leaf fell open in four clean pieces where the lesson had asked for one.

The room went quiet in the particular way rooms do when something has happened that the rules did not account for. Tetsuna stopped walking. She did not smile — smiling was a kind of erosion — but she stood over his desk a long moment, weighing something behind her eyes.

In the doorway, a thin scholarly man who had not been scheduled to watch this class at all spoke softly, to no one. "Earth, Wind, and Fire," said Ryūen-sensei. "All three. In a child." He said it the way other men said the names of the dead.

At the back of the room, a broad, sun-darkened boy snapped his pencil clean in half and did not seem to notice he had done it.

## The Stair
If the morning made Iwao something the instructors wrote careful notes about, the afternoon reminded everyone that he was eleven.

Conditioning was honest work. Tetsuna sent them up the inner cliff-stair under full packs — pure leg, pure lung, no chakra tricks allowed — and on the stair Iwao's gifts deserted him completely. His hands could split a leaf into the future; his legs were only legs. He came in near the back, chest heaving, the taste of stone-dust in his throat, while ahead of him that broad boy crested the top step barely winded and turned to watch the heir straggle in.

Something curdled in the boy's face. He had spent his whole life climbing for the air at the top of that stair. He had just watched the universe hand a leaf-cut to someone for free.

His name was Ganryū, and at the noon break he found Iwao at the practice posts.

"The wind cut that leaf." He said it quietly, which was worse than shouting. "Not you. Get used to earning things, heir." He spat the title like a seed and walked off.

Iwao didn't argue. He never could find the words for it — *I didn't ask for this, I'd give some of it away if I could* — and so he said nothing, and his silence landed on Ganryū like pity, and made everything worse. A round-faced girl dropped down beside him anyway, uninvited, and started talking enough for the both of them; her name was Akemi, and she could feel his chakra from across the yard, she said, like a storm with the lid still on. A quiet boy named Tobari wordlessly broke a rice-ball in half and held out the larger piece. It was not nothing. He was learning that nothing was a smaller word than people made it sound.

## The Legend That Wasn't Dead
History was the lesson Iwao loved and the one he was best at. When Ryūen asked who could name the bloodline that had ground an army into dust at the Battle of the Border, Iwao's hand was up before he had decided to raise it, and the answer poured out of him whole — the lineage, the seals, the way the technique folded three natures into a single frame of pure undoing and let nothing caught inside it stay a *thing*.

He recited it the way some children recite the names of monsters they are sure live under the bed. Because somewhere in the reciting the cold understanding arrived and sat down under his ribs to stay: the man who had done this was not a legend in a scroll. He was alive. He was the Third Tsuchikage. He was, this very moment, somewhere above all their heads in this same mountain — and the whole village kept searching Iwao's small face for his.

## The Mark, and the Wind
Kanna-sensei chalked a target the size of a coin on the far wall of the range and said that hitting it once by week's end would earn a mark of merit. Iwao threw on the first breath and buried the shuriken dead in the center of the chalk. Then he stood there feeling faintly ill, because it had not felt like skill. It had felt like his hands simply knew. Kanna wrote something down and said nothing. Three posts over, Ganryū threw until his knuckles split, and kept throwing.

When the others had gone, Ryūen kept him back — to teach, he said, but really to *watch*. He would not let Iwao reach for the bloodline. "Small natures first. In the hands, before the blood. Show me wind."

So Iwao showed him wind. He molded his first real technique, the cutting weasel-wind off his fingertips, and the straw training dummy at the end of the range came apart in a spiral of slashing air — utterly, completely, far past anything the exercise had asked for. And the molding of that single technique left him swaying, the world graying at its edges, his chakra scraped down to almost nothing.

This was the shape of Iwao, on the first day: a cannon with a thimble of powder. Ryūen watched the straw settle and did not look delighted. He looked like a man who had just confirmed something he had been afraid of for a long time. "Enough," he said quietly. "That's plenty of wind for one boy in one day."

## The Stone You Carry
At dinner his father came home grey with dust, washed to the elbow, and over the stone-pot stew Mai told everything — the leaf, the shuriken, *the wind that ate a dummy.* The table went quiet. Gennai set down his bowl with the great care of a man who handled heavy things for a living.

"A bloodline isn't a crown, boy." He held his son's eyes. "It's a heavier stone. The only question that has ever mattered about a heavy stone is whether the man carrying it crushes the people walking beside him." A pause that Iwao would carry for the rest of his life. "So don't."

Later, washing bowls, Tsuru finally said the thing she had held behind her teeth all day. In the market they had spoken her son's name in the same breath as the Tsuchikage's. He was *eleven.* She made him promise to come straight home tomorrow. It was not a question, and he did not pretend it was one.

## The Eye Above
He slept the dreamless sleep of a boy who has run a cliff-stair, with Mai curled warm against his side, and never knew the village moved around him while he did. He never knew that the headmaster left a quiet note in the instructors' box, or that Ryūen sat up late over a field report thirty years old.

And he could not have known that on the highest terrace of the Hidden Stone a small, stooped old man stood with his hands folded behind his back, looking down through the dark at one particular window.

"Earth, Wind, and Fire," Ōnoki said to the wind, which took the words and scattered them like everything else. "So. It skips to the boy."

He did not sound pleased. He did not sound displeased. He sounded like a mountain, settling — certain it would outlast anything that climbed it, and not at all certain that, this once, it wanted to.

Below him, the heir of the Hidden Stone slept on, and dreamed of nothing, and did not yet know he had begun to climb.
