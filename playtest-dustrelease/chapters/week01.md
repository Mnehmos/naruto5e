TITLE: Chapter 1 — The Weight of Stone
DATES: Academy, Year One · Days 1–7 · Iwagakure, the Hidden Stone
---
The Hidden Stone did not sleep so much as settle, the way a mountain settles — slowly, with great weight, certain it would outlast anything that climbed it. From the high terraces of Iwagakure the village fell away in tiers of ochre rock, bridges strung between spires like the threads of some patient spider, and over all of it the wind came down off the cliffs carrying grit fine as flour.

Iwao tasted that grit on his teeth every morning of his life. He was eleven years old, narrow-shouldered, with his mother's careful eyes and a name that meant nothing more or less than *rock*. And on the first day of the Academy, when the instructors lined the new students against the wall of the training yard, he learned that he was not nothing at all.

He was, the whole village seemed to have decided before he ever threw a kunai, **everything**.

## The Molding

"Mold your chakra to the seal and hold it. Hold it until I tell you to stop, or until you faint. Whichever comes first." Tetsuna-sensei was built like a retaining wall and spoke like one too — flat, immovable, vaguely disapproving of erosion. She walked the line of children with her hands behind her back.

Most of the class managed a flicker. A few managed a steady glow. When she reached Iwao, he laced his fingers into the Ram and *reached* — the way breathing is a kind of reaching once you think about it — and the chakra came so cleanly, so completely, that the air above his hands shivered with three colors at once: the brown of turned earth, the pale of moving air, the red of something waiting to burn.

The roll of it was effortless. *(Chakra Control — 24 vs DC 12, a clean success, modifier +5.)* Tetsuna-sensei stood over him a long moment. She did not smile. But she stopped walking.

"Earth, Wind, and Fire," murmured Ryūen-sensei from the doorway, the chakra-theory instructor, a thin scholarly man who had not been scheduled to watch this class at all. "All three. In a child." He said it the way other men said the names of the dead.

## The Stumble

If the morning made Iwao a legend, the afternoon reminded him he was a boy.

The obstacle run wound up the cliff stair and back — rope, wall, beam, drop. Iwao was clever and quick of hand but his legs were only legs, and on the slick beam his foot betrayed him and he went down hard onto the packed clay, scattering chalk and dignity in equal measure. *(Athletics — 11 vs DC 12. Failure. By a single point.)*

Laughter — but one laugh louder than the rest. **Ganryū.** Broad, sun-darkened, the son of a respected stonemason family, top of the entrance scores until that morning's chakra drill had quietly rearranged the world. He hauled himself over the wall Iwao had stumbled past, landed clean, and looked down at the boy in the chalk.

"They say the wind gave you everything," Ganryū said, not cruelly, which was worse than cruelly. "Funny. It didn't give you *that*." He jerked his chin at the beam. "I had to earn the beam. Get used to earning things, *heir*." He spat the title like a seed.

A hand appeared in Iwao's vision — small, ink-stained, attached to a round-faced girl with a sensor's too-bright awareness. **Akemi.** "Don't mind him," she said, hauling Iwao up. "Your chakra felt like a storm with the lid still on. I felt it from the *other side of the yard*." She grinned. "That's not nothing. That's just not balanced yet."

## The Legend and the Daydream

History was the one lecture Iwao loved, and the one he was best at. When Ryūen-sensei asked who could name the Kekkei Genkai that had ground an army to dust at the Battle of the Border, three hands went up. Iwao's answer was the long one — the lineage, the seals, the way **Dust Release, Jinton**, folds three natures into a frame of pure dissolution and lets nothing inside it remain a thing. *(History — 19 vs DC 13. Success.)* He recited it the way some children recite the names of monsters they are afraid live under the bed.

Because the man who had done it — the man whose blood the village now searched Iwao's face for — was not dead. He was the **Third Tsuchikage**. He was alive. He was *here*.

Iwao knew all of it, every date and every seal. What he could not do, that first week, was sit still through written theory afterward while his mind kept drifting up the cliffs to a small old man he had never met. *(Intelligence — 6 vs DC 12. Failure. The genius, daydreaming, left half the page blank.)* Tetsuna-sensei collected the paper without a word, which was the loudest word she had.

## Watched

That night Iwao slept the dead, dreamless sleep of an eleven-year-old who has run a cliff stair, and did not know that the village moved around him while he did. *(Long rest — pools full, Will of Fire renewed.)*

He did not know that the Academy headmaster, **Sōun**, left a note in the instructors' box that read *keep his marks ordinary in the ledger; extraordinary travels too fast.* He did not know that Ryūen-sensei sat late re-reading a thirty-year-old field report. And he did not know — could not have known — that on the highest terrace, a small figure no taller than the genin he watched stood with his hands folded behind a stooped back, looking down through the dark at one particular window.

*(World tick, magnitude medium: the Tsuchikage Ōnoki "watches over Iwao." Iwagakure standing +2 → 7.)*

"Earth, Wind, and Fire," the old man said to no one. The wind took it off the cliff and scattered it like everything else. "So. It skips to the boy." He did not sound pleased. He did not sound displeased. He sounded like a mountain, settling — certain it would outlast anything that climbed it, and not at all certain that, this once, it wanted to.

Far below, in a window the old man would not name aloud, the heir of the Hidden Stone slept on, and dreamed of nothing, and did not yet know he had begun to climb.
